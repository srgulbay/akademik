## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Principal findings

This systematic synthesis of 340 records covering 2003-2025 establishes that QS in *Acinetobacter baumannii* is supported by a mature, multi-modal evidence base. The molecular architecture of the AbaI/AbaR autoinducer system has been delineated from biochemistry through gene regulation [Niu_2008; Bhargava_2010; Sun_2021; Mayer_2020]. QS regulates biofilm formation, surface-associated motility, antibiotic tolerance and a constellation of virulence determinants (§3.3). A heterogeneous but expanding QSI pipeline spans synthetic AHL analogues [Stacy_2012; Stacy_2012_2], natural products [Bhargava_2015; Lin_2023; Cui_2025; Santajit_2025], QQ enzymes [Chow_2013; Mayer_2018; Lpez_2017_2; Bergonzi_2018; Vogel_2022] and engineered phages [Li_2024; Su_2025; deVilliersdelaNoue_2025; Arazi_2025]. In vivo proof-of-concept exists in *Galleria*, murine pneumonia, sepsis and wound models (§3.5), and a small but informative clinical-association literature links QS gene expression to disease severity in CRAB bacteraemia and ventilator-associated pneumonia (§3.6). What the corpus does *not* yet contain is decisive: only one published interventional clinical trial of a QS-targeting modality (a phage compassionate-use programme) and no Phase II-III randomised data for any small-molecule QSI. Two decades of mechanistic work have not yet translated into a single licensed adjunct.

### 4.2 Mechanistic consensus and controversies

A clear consensus is that AbaI is the dominant — and likely sole — LuxI-family synthase in clinical *A. baumannii*, that 3-OH-C12-HSL is its principal product [Niu_2008; Chan_2014; Mayer_2018], and that isogenic *abaI*/*abaR* mutants show reproducibly attenuated biofilm and virulence [Sun_2021; Mayer_2020; Luo_2015]. Three controversies remain. *First, is AbaR the master regulator?* Transcriptomic studies in clinical isolates show that QS output is heavily filtered through BfmRS, AdeRS, PmrAB and global regulators (DksA, Hfq) [Xie_2025; RumboFeal_2013; Cui_2022; Kim_2021; Wiradiputra_2025]; the current model is polygenic, with AbaR a node rather than a master switch. *Second, do AHL and indole signals act in parallel or in sequence?* The Cui group's work [Cui_2022; Cui_2023; Cui_2025; Cui_2025_2] and [Li_2025] reframe indole as an interspecies signal acting through a response regulator distinct from AbaR, with at least partial parallelism and shared downstream effectors (*csu*, *bap*). *Third, what is the role of c-di-GMP and cAMP?* [Harkova_2024] and [Cui_2025_2] frame nucleotide second messengers as global virulence regulators interfacing with QS, but the molecular grammar — which adenylate cyclases, which phosphodiesterases, which sensor inputs — is only beginning to be mapped. Single-cell reconciliation of AbaIR transcriptomics with second-messenger dynamics is a clear research priority.

### 4.3 Translational pipeline assessment

Four bottlenecks explain why no QSI has reached Phase II for *A. baumannii*. *Target validation versus clinical relevance*: in vitro validation is strong, but the few studies measuring 3-OH-C12-HSL in patient samples [Chan_2014] showed detection in some but not all sputum samples; whether AbaIR is engaged during early-stage human infection — when intervention is most useful — is inferential. *PK/PD gaps*: for natural-product QSIs, demonstrated effective concentrations (25-200 µg/mL) [Cui_2025; Lin_2023; Koshak_2024; Elfaky_2024] exceed plasma C_max for orally dosed polyphenols by one to two orders of magnitude; no natural-product QSI has been advanced to a defined human PK study. *Formulation and delivery*: encapsulation strategies [Nosair_2025; Hetta_2021; Pourhajibagher_2023; DudaMadej_2025] improve apparent activity but introduce new regulatory burdens, with manufacturable scale-up data rare. *Phage therapy is the nearest-clinic option*: lytic phages and engineered cocktails have the clearest clinical-translation pathway [deVilliersdelaNoue_2025; Essam_2025; Arazi_2025; Su_2025; Li_2024; Sitthisak_2023; Styles_2020]. Several phages target capsular structures whose expression is QS- and BfmRS-regulated, so phage activity intersects QS biology even when not explicitly designed as a QS intervention. *Adjunctive combination is the realistic horizon*: the strongest in vivo signals come from QSI-antibiotic combinations — polymyxin B with ceftazidime against biofilms [Li_2022], colistin with phage-encoded peptides [Rothong_2024], erythromycin sensitising CRAB [Dong_2024], and streptomycin as a serendipitous adjuvant [Saroj_2013]. [Beasley_2025] explicitly frames QSIs as therapy enhancers rather than monotherapies, and this framing best fits the available data.

### 4.4 Polymicrobial context and comparator pathogens

A striking feature of the corpus is that 203 of 340 records (59.7%) frame their work in a multi-species or ESKAPE context [Bhargava_2012; Wheeler_2025; Sorenson_2025; Chong_2025; Vinitha_2025; Hetta_2025]. A species-specific QSI deployed clinically may have its activity attenuated by cross-acting AHLs from co-colonising species — an effect largely unexamined in current preclinical models. Broader-spectrum QQ enzymes may have an advantage over narrow small-molecule inhibitors [Vogel_2022; Bergonzi_2018; Chow_2013]. Mechanistically, *A. baumannii* QS differs from the canonical *P. aeruginosa* (Las/Rhl/PQS), *S. aureus* (Agr) and streptococcal (ComX/CSP) systems in three respects: it possesses a single LuxI synthase but compensates with extensive TCS and second-messenger integration (more polygenic, less hierarchical); it secretes and responds to indole as a signal [Cui_2022; Li_2025]; and it carries unusual endogenous QQ activity in the same organism that produces AHL [Mayer_2018; Lpez_2017_2]. Strategies validated in *P. aeruginosa* therefore do not transfer directly because the AbaR ligand-binding pocket differs structurally [BellI_2025].

### 4.5 Limitations of this review

Six limitations should be considered. *Database scope*: we did not search Scopus, Web of Science, Embase or Cochrane Library; potentially relevant non-MEDLINE-indexed records may have been missed. Cross-database corroboration between PubMed, OpenAlex and Crossref (264 of 340 PubMed records re-discovered by ≥1 external source) suggests indexing coverage was substantial. *Language restriction*: English-only inclusion may under-represent regional reports from East Asia and Latin America. *Review-process design*: title/abstract and full-text screening used a structured protocol with internal consistency checks; risk-of-bias was assessed at the field level rather than per-study, and per-study GRADE certainty was not applied. *Heterogeneity*: variation in outcome definitions, biofilm-assay endpoints, sub-MIC choices and strain background precluded pooled meta-analysis; effect sizes are summarised narratively (§3.8, Table 2). *Publication bias*: formal funnel-plot assessment was not feasible due to this heterogeneity, but the positive-result skew identified in §3.9.2 should temper interpretation of summary effect ranges. *Lack of patient-level data*: only 15 clinical-association studies were identified, most retrospective and single-centre. Despite these limitations, the convergence of in vitro mechanistic, in vivo proof-of-concept, omics and clinical-association data constitutes a multi-modal evidence base whose qualitative direction is robust to plausible refinements in search and screening.

### 4.6 Future research priorities

Seven priorities follow from this synthesis: (1) solve the AbaR structure (cryo-EM or X-ray, ideally co-crystallised with 3-OH-C12-HSL or an antagonist) to enable structure-based design beyond current model-dependent docking [BellI_2025; Aruwa_2025; Alagesan_2025]; (2) adopt community-consensus biofilm assays with orthogonal readouts and a defined strain panel including AB5075 and contemporary CRAB isolates; (3) test QS biomarkers (AHL, indole concentrations) prospectively in CRAB cohorts (VAP, burn-wound, bacteraemia) to determine severity and treatment-response correlations, building on [Chan_2014] and [Carpenito_2025]; (4) advance engineered probiotics and topical QQ-enzyme formulations (PvdQ, AaL, AidA) with manufacturability, stability and immunogenicity studies [Vogel_2022; Bergonzi_2018; Lpez_2017_2]; (5) run Phase I trials of rationally designed two-to-four-phage cocktails for MDR pneumonia and wound infections [deVilliersdelaNoue_2025; Su_2025; Sitthisak_2023; Arazi_2025]; (6) adopt the unified minimum reporting dataset (§3.9.5; Supplementary S9.4) backed by journal policy; (7) replace monomicrobial models with defined two- to three-species consortia to test QSI efficacy under realistic co-colonisation [Bhargava_2012; Wheeler_2025].

## 5. Conclusion

Twenty-two years of mechanistic, omics and preclinical evidence establish that QS is a credible therapeutic target in *Acinetobacter baumannii*. The AbaI/AbaR axis, modulated by BfmRS, AdeRS, PmrAB and indole signalling, governs biofilm formation, motility, antibiotic tolerance and virulence determinants that together account for much of the pathogen's clinical recalcitrance. Translation has nevertheless stalled at the preclinical-clinical interface: no small-molecule QSI has entered Phase II evaluation, and clinical evidence remains largely associational. The strongest case from this corpus is for **adjunctive** rather than standalone QSI therapy, deploying QSIs and QQ enzymes to sensitise *A. baumannii* to existing antibiotics or to attenuate biofilm-mediated tolerance during host clearance. Phage therapy, including QS-intersecting capsule-targeting phages, is the most clinically advanced QS-modulating modality and is ready for properly designed Phase I trials in defined indications. The principal translational bottleneck is no longer target validation — which is robust — but rather dosing, formulation, clinical pharmacokinetics and standardised reporting. A unified minimum-information dataset for *A. baumannii* QS studies, combined with structured biomarker validation in burn-wound, VAP and bacteraemia patient cohorts, would substantially accelerate the field. The next five years should prioritise solving the AbaR structure, running Phase I phage-cocktail trials, validating QS biomarkers in patient samples, and adopting community-standard reporting. With these advances, QS-directed therapy is plausibly within reach as a clinical adjunct for multidrug-resistant *A. baumannii* infection within the coming decade.
