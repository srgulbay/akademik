## 3.9 Methodological Quality and Risk of Bias

### 3.9.1 Reporting standards adherence

Across the 228 full-text records eligible for structured claim extraction, adherence to discipline-specific reporting frameworks was uneven (Figure 5). In vitro QSI-screening and biofilm-phenotyping papers (n = 33 with full text) frequently omitted inoculum size at biofilm initiation, medium cation composition, the exact wavelength used in crystal-violet quantification and the rationale for sub-MIC concentrations; only a minority (e.g., Mayer_2020; Stacy_2012; Lin_2023) approached the developing CRIS standard. Animal studies (n = 50) reported group sizes universally, but power-calculation justification was rare (<10%), randomisation was stated in roughly one-third, blinding of outcome assessment in fewer than a quarter, and attrition was inconsistently disclosed; foundational invertebrate work [Peleg_2008; Peleg_2009] predates ARRIVE 2.0, and more recent papers [Cui_2025; Li_2025; Nosair_2025; Hetta_2021] describe humane endpoints but rarely report allocation sequences. Computational studies (n = 10 with full text) typically used a single docking engine without consensus scoring or decoy controls, and MD simulation length (50-100 ns) was below the 200-500 ns benchmark for transcription-regulator binding [BellI_2025; Aruwa_2025; Jha_2024]. Omics studies (n = 61) showed comparatively strong sequencing data deposition (~70% in GEO/SRA/ENA) but lower proteomics deposition (~50%, PRIDE/ProteomeXchange) and the weakest metabolomics deposition.

### 3.9.2 Risk-of-bias patterns

Field-level patterns of bias were assessed using domain-adapted SYRCLE (in vivo) and modified ROBINS-I (in vitro and clinical association) frameworks. Five recurrent patterns are: *selection bias* — clinical-isolate panels often described only as "MDR" or "CRAB" without susceptibility, sequence type or capsular type, with convenience sampling from single tertiary centres dominating [Zhang_2025; Zhu_2022; Liu_2016_2; FernndezVzquez_2023]; *performance bias* — lack of blinding in biofilm and virulence scoring is the norm, with only a small minority [Cui_2025] explicitly reporting blinded plate or histopathology scorers; *detection bias* — subjective scoring of crystal-violet-stained microtitre plates without orthogonal readouts (XTT, CLSM, qPCR) is a recurring weakness, and triangulating studies [Mayer_2020; Cui_2022; Liu_2020] were qualitatively more robust; *reporting bias* — of 80 papers categorised as "QSI discovery", we found only four explicitly null or negative reports, and the absence of pre-registered "tested but inactive" supplementary tables distorts the apparent translational signal; and *conflicts of interest* — several natural-product papers were funded by indigenous-medicinal-plant programmes, and the close alignment of funder priorities with reported "potent inhibition" warrants caution.

### 3.9.3 Heterogeneity sources

Four heterogeneity sources constrained quantitative synthesis. *Strain diversity*: ATCC 17978 dominates (47.8%), followed by ATCC 19606 (18.0%), AB5075 (9.6%) and AYE (5.3%); the comparatively non-virulent ATCC 17978 may not generalise to contemporary AB5075-like MDR isolates, and ~60% of clinical-isolate panels were used only once. *Biofilm assay variation*: crystal violet (biomass), XTT/MTT (metabolic activity), CLSM (architecture and viability) and qPCR of biofilm markers measure different biological properties and are not interconvertible. *MIC/susceptibility methodology*: CLSI versus EUCAST breakpoints and inoculum differences yielded MIC ranges varying by 2-4-fold dilutions between laboratories. *Sub-MIC dose range*: anti-biofilm testing spanned 1/2 to 1/64 MIC across the corpus, rendering "potent QS inhibition" claims at unspecified fractional MIC non-comparable.

### 3.9.4 Down-weighting in narrative synthesis

In framing the synthesis (§3.3-3.6) we down-weighted four categories of report whose characteristics weaken contribution to effect-size claims: in silico-only studies without wet-lab validation (~15 records); crystal-violet-only natural-extract screens without compound characterisation or growth-curve controls; animal studies without group-size justification, randomisation or attrition reporting; and clinical-isolate studies without susceptibility profiles or sequence-type information. Down-weighting did not equate to refutation, and qualitative directions of effect were robust to inclusion or exclusion of these records.

### 3.9.5 A minimum reporting dataset

To improve cross-study comparability and to enable future quantitative synthesis, we propose a minimum reporting dataset for *A. baumannii* QS studies, summarised in Supplementary S9.4 and incorporating: (i) full strain provenance with susceptibility, capsular and sequence-type information; (ii) culture medium with explicit cation supplementation; (iii) inoculum density and growth-phase definition; (iv) at least two orthogonal biofilm readouts; (v) explicit fractional MIC for all sub-inhibitory testing; (vi) ARRIVE 2.0-compliant in vivo methods (randomisation, blinded outcome assessment, sample-size justification, humane endpoints); (vii) raw-data deposition in GEO/SRA/ENA or PRIDE/ProteomeXchange; (viii) docking with decoy controls and ≥200 ns MD where structural-binding claims are central; and (ix) deposition of negative/inactive screening data to mitigate positive-result publication bias. Journal-policy adoption would substantially improve cumulative interpretability of *A. baumannii* QS research.
