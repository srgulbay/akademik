## 3.3 The *A. baumannii* Quorum-Sensing Molecular Network

Quorum sensing (QS) in *Acinetobacter baumannii* is now understood as a multi-layered regulatory web rather than a single LuxI/LuxR circuit. The N-acyl-homoserine lactone (AHL) axis identified two decades ago remains the canonical core, but contemporary work has integrated it with an indole-receptor system, three nucleotide second-messenger pools, several two-component systems (TCSs), and a small set of accessory regulators (AbaM, ABUW_1132, CavA/VfrAb, DksA, ddrR) that together coordinate biofilm, efflux, surface and motility phenotypes [Cui 2025_2; Niu 2008; Bhargava 2010]. The following sub-sections summarise the components, signal chemistry, cross-regulation and unresolved questions of this network as currently mapped in the 218 full-text and 122 abstract-only studies included in this review.

### 3.3.1 The abaI/abaR axis: the canonical LuxI/LuxR-type system

The founding component of the network is the *abaI*/*abaR* pair, identified in *A. baumannii* strain M2 by Niu and colleagues [Niu 2008]. AbaI is a LuxI-family acyl-homoserine lactone synthase predicted to be the sole AHL synthase encoded by the species; AbaR is the cognate LuxR-family transcriptional regulator [Niu 2008; Bhargava 2010; Cui 2025_2]. A *lux*-box-like sequence (CTGTAAATTCTTACAG) located ~67 bp upstream of *abaI* mediates a positive-feedback loop in which the AHL–AbaR complex activates further *abaI* transcription, producing the cell-density-dependent step characteristic of LuxI/LuxR circuits [Cui 2025_2]. A targeted *abaI*::Km mutant in strain M2 failed to produce detectable AHLs and showed approximately 40% reduction in biofilm biomass relative to wild-type, with subsequent independent reproductions confirming reduced surface motility, attenuated virulence in *Galleria* and murine models, and increased serum susceptibility for ∆*abaI* and ∆*abaIR* derivatives [Niu 2008; Sun 2021_2; Pumirat 2024]. Phylogenetic analyses suggest that *abaI* and *abaR* were horizontally acquired, with closest similarity to homologues in *Halothiobacillus neapolitanus* rather than the related *Burkholderia* clade [Bhargava 2010].

Mechanistically, structural and binding studies of AbaR confirm conventional LuxR-type architecture: an N-terminal autoinducer-binding domain plus a C-terminal helix-turn-helix DNA-binding domain that engages target promoters in the AHL-bound state [Cui 2025_2; Sun 2021]. Transcriptome profiling of an *abaR* knockdown identified scores of differentially expressed loci including A1S_3544, A1S_0849, A1S_3867 and A1S_3011, indicating that AbaR has a regulon well beyond *abaI* itself [Sun 2021]. The accessory regulator AbaM, encoded immediately downstream of *abaI*, acts as a brake on AHL output; loss of *abaM* increases AHL titres, surface motility and biofilm biomass, while reducing virulence — establishing it as a paradoxical attenuator that uncouples QS-driven biofilm from systemic virulence [Lpez-Martn 2021]. Two further upstream modulators have been described: the LysR-type regulator ABUW_1132, which positively regulates *abaIR* transcription (its deletion causes a 16-fold drop in 3-OH-C12-HSL and switches the VIR-O virulent variant to the AV-T avirulent variant) [Tierney 2021]; and the CRISPR-Cas system, which can target endogenous *abaI* and thereby alters antibiotic susceptibility [Wang 2022_2].

*Mechanistic schema (Figure 3A, suggested):* AbaI condenses S-adenosyl-methionine with a 3-hydroxydodecanoyl-ACP donor to synthesise 3-OH-C12-HSL → AHL diffuses across the membrane → at threshold density it binds AbaR → AbaR-AHL homodimer recognises the *lux*-box upstream of *abaI* and additional QS-target promoters → coordinated activation of biofilm, motility, efflux and virulence regulons → AbaM and AidA (see 3.3.7) provide feedback dampening, while ABUW_1132 (LysR) and (p)ppGpp/RelA provide nutritional and stress-state inputs.

### 3.3.2 Signal chemistry: 3-OH-C12-HSL and its analogues

Mass-spectrometric profiling of *A. baumannii* M2 supernatants identified N-(3-hydroxydodecanoyl)-L-homoserine lactone (3-OH-C12-HSL; also written OH-C12 or OHdDHL) as the dominant AHL, accompanied by minor amounts of unsubstituted C10-HSL and C12-HSL, 3-hydroxy-C10-HSL, unsaturated 3-oxo-C11-HSL, and C14-HSL [Niu 2008; Cui 2025_2]. Subsequent surveys of clinical and environmental *Acinetobacter* isolates have shown that AHL chain length (C6–C14) and substitution patterns vary between strains and lineages but converge on medium- to long-chain 3-hydroxy species, with short-chain (C6, C8) AHLs detected at sub-nM levels in ATCC 17978 supernatants [Gonzlez 2009; Mayer 2018; Bitrian 2012]. Recent LC-MS/MS quantification in plasma from burn patients with *A. baumannii* septic shock demonstrated that 3-OH-C12-HSL is detectable in vivo, supporting clinical relevance of the signal [Carpenito 2025].

Stereochemistry studies by Garner et al. and Stacy et al. found that, unusually for LuxR systems, AbaR responds nearly identically to (R)- and (S)-3-OH-C12-HSL (EC50 0.67 and 0.82 μM respectively) — a stereochemical insignificance that contrasts with strict R-preference in *P. aeruginosa* LasR and *P. putida* PpuR [Garner 2012; Stacy 2012]. Both groups also showed that synthetic AHL analogues bearing aromatic head-groups or triazole linkages function as AbaR antagonists in *A. baumannii* reporter strains, reducing motility and biofilm formation [Stacy 2012; Stacy 2012_2]. These observations underpin current AbaR-targeted lead chemistry, including computational lead identification efforts [BellI 2025] and natural-product screens with carnosol, glabridin, eugenol, peppermint and *Salvia* essential oils, rhamnolipid, palmitoleic/myristoleic and nervonic/oleic acids [Cui 2025; Lin 2023; Ribeiro 2024; MajidiFard 2025; Masoomi 2025; Nicol 2018; Khadke 2021; Firdose 2024; Bhargava 2015]. AHL signalling is environmentally tunable: exposure to blue light reduces *abaI* transcription approximately 38-fold compared with darkness in ATCC 17978, and human serum albumin sequesters 3-OH-C12-HSL to suppress AHL output [Tuttobene 2021; Pimentel 2021; Pimentel 2021_2]. AHL pools can also be erased enzymatically: the lactonases MomL, AaL and AidA, and the *P. aeruginosa* acylase PvdQ, all degrade 3-OH-C12-HSL and attenuate biofilm formation in *A. baumannii* [Zhang 2017; Bergonzi 2018; Lpez 2017_2; Vogel 2022; Chow 2013; Mayer 2018; Mayer 2020].

### 3.3.3 Cross-regulation with two-component systems (BfmRS, AdeRS, PmrAB)

The AbaI/AbaR axis does not function in isolation: it is wired in parallel to several TCSs whose sensors detect envelope, salt, divalent-cation and antibiotic stress.

The BfmRS TCS is the best-characterised QS partner. In ATCC 19606, BfmR/BfmS expression rises in response to exogenous C6-HSL (~1.33-fold) and tracks the up-regulation of the chaperone-usher *csuA/BABCDE* operon, suggesting that QS feeds into BfmRS-mediated pilus assembly [Luo 2015]. Kim and colleagues subsequently mapped a complementary regulatory loop in which BfmRS and the AbaIR system jointly govern virulence-associated genes, with reciprocal modulation under shaking vs. static growth and AHL supplementation [Kim 2022]. Loss of the (p)ppGpp-binding RNA-polymerase cofactor DksA also de-represses *bfmR/S* and *csuC/D/E*, linking the stringent response to QS-controlled biofilm machinery [Kim 2021]. Inhibition of BfmR (RstA) by *Daniellia oliveri* phytochemicals has been proposed as a QS-targeted strategy [Aruwa 2025].

The AdeRS TCS controls expression of the AdeABC RND efflux pump and, in turn, both influences and is influenced by QS. AdeABC-deficient strains (∆*adeB*) show elevated AHL output, type-VI secretion and biofilm formation, particularly in bile-salt-containing media [Lpez 2017]. Conversely, comparative deletion analyses of AdeABC, AdeFGH and AdeIJK in the AYE reference strain demonstrate that ∆*adeRS* shows the strongest motility phenotype (24 mm vs. 13 mm wild-type) and that AdeFGH overexpression is coupled to AHL-stimulated biofilm formation [Xie 2025; He 2015]. The CheA/Y-like hybrid two-component regulator A1S_2811 is required for AbaIR-dependent surface motility and biofilm formation in ATCC 17978, and exogenous N-(3-oxodecanoyl)-L-HSL rescues the surface-motility defect of the ∆A1S_2811 strain, providing direct genetic evidence that AHL signalling converges on chemotaxis-type signal transduction [Chen 2017]. The PmrAB system, which governs lipid-A modifications relevant to colistin resistance, is repeatedly co-listed with *abaI*/*abaR* in transcriptomic surveys of CRAB and colistin-treated isolates, but a mechanistic link between PmrAB and the AHL circuit has not yet been established in *A. baumannii* and remains a gap (see 3.3.7) [YousefiNojookambari 2024; Shenkutie 2022].

### 3.3.4 QS-dependent regulons

Phenotypic and transcriptomic studies have defined four broad output modules of the *A. baumannii* QS network.

**Biofilm matrix (*csu*, *bap*, *pgaABCD*).** AHL signalling and the indole-responsive regulator AbiR converge on the chaperone-usher *csuA/BABCDE* operon, which assembles the type-I pilus required for surface attachment. *csuA/B* and *pilW* are direct AbiR targets in EMSA, and loss of AbiR reduces biofilm by ~41% [Cui 2023; Cui 2025; Cui 2025_2]. The biofilm-associated protein Bap is co-listed with *abaI/abaR* in virulence-gene panels of clinical isolates and is up-regulated in biofilm-state transcriptomes [Mendes 2023; Liu 2024; Vinitha 2025; Rumbo-Feal 2013]. The poly-β-1,6-N-acetyl-glucosamine (PNAG) synthesis locus *pgaABCD* tracks *abaI* in burn-wound isolates (real-time PCR copy numbers of *pgaD* and *abaI* rise in tandem during biofilm maturation) [HatamiMoghadam 2018; Cui 2025_2].

**Efflux pumps (*adeABC*, *adeFGH*, *adeIJK*).** AdeFGH over-expression accompanies AHL-driven biofilm induction under sub-MIC levofloxacin or meropenem, linking QS to phenotypic antibiotic tolerance [He 2015; Xie 2025]. AdeABC and AdeIJK contribute to motility and biofilm-state susceptibility shifts; ∆*adeB* mutants display ~41% reduction in biofilm formation and elevated AHL output, indicating bidirectional cross-talk [Xie 2025; Lpez 2017].

**Outer-membrane protein A (*ompA*).** OmpA, which trafficks via outer-membrane vesicles (OMVs) to deliver virulence factors into host cells and facilitates inter-bacterial AHL exchange, is included in the QS-regulated regulon of *A. baumannii*, with reduced *ompA* expression in *abaI*-disrupted backgrounds and in QSI-treated cultures [Cui 2025_2; Vinitha 2025; Kostoulias 2015; Guo 2024].

**Iron acquisition (acinetobactin).** The acinetobactin biosynthesis and uptake loci, together with the BauA receptor and TonB-dependent transport apparatus, are co-regulated with QS genes in transcriptomic and adaptive-evolution datasets [Ghavanloughajar 2025; Vinitha 2025; Law 2022; Chen 2024]. Pimentel et al. showed that human pleural fluid and HSA exposure simultaneously down-regulate *abaI*, *abaR* and iron-uptake genes, implying convergent host-factor sensing [Pimentel 2021; Pimentel 2021_2]. Conversely, the AbaR regulon includes loci encoding capsule and siderophore biosynthesis [Sun 2021; Sun 2021_2; Tierney 2021].

### 3.3.5 Nucleotide second messengers (c-di-GMP, ppGpp, 3′,5′-cAMP) as QS modulators

Three intracellular nucleotide second messengers feed into the QS network. The *A. baumannii* ATCC 17978 genome encodes 12 candidate GGDEF/EAL domain proteins that synthesise or degrade c-di-GMP; A1S_1695, A1S_2506 and A1S_3692 act as diguanylate cyclases, and A1S_1254 is a phosphodiesterase, with deletions altering biofilm, motility and virulence [Cui 2025_2]. The c-di-GMP effector EF-P (A1S_2419) couples the messenger to translational rescue of proline-rich proteins, providing a unique mode of QS-relevant output [Cui 2025_2]. (p)ppGpp synthesised by the RelA homologue ABUW_3302 (RelAAb) modulates antibiotic susceptibility, biofilm formation and virulence; importantly, RelAAb represses ABUW_1132, which itself activates *abaIR* — placing the stringent response upstream of the AHL circuit [Prez-Varela 2020; Tierney 2021; Cui 2025_2]. The adenylate cyclase CavA produces 3′,5′-cAMP, which binds the Vfr-like receptor VfrAb; remarkably, in *A. baumannii* AB5075 cAMP positively (not inversely) regulates c-di-GMP and represses *abaI* transcription, placing 3′,5′-cAMP at the apex of a hierarchical cascade [Harkova 2024; Cui 2025_2].

*Mechanistic schema (Figure 3B, suggested):* Environmental cue (nutrient or stress) → CavA produces 3′,5′-cAMP → VfrAb represses *abaI* while activating diguanylate cyclases → c-di-GMP rises → EF-P enhances translation of Pro-rich biofilm proteins; in parallel, nutrient starvation → RelAAb produces (p)ppGpp → represses ABUW_1132 → reduces *abaIR* transcription → biofilm/motility switch. Output: integration of cell density (AHL), intracellular energy state ((p)ppGpp), and carbon/host cue (cAMP) at a single transcriptional decision point.

### 3.3.6 Polymicrobial signal cross-talk

*A. baumannii* shares niches (lung, wound, catheter biofilms) with *P. aeruginosa*, *S. aureus* and *Candida* spp., and inter-species QS cross-talk is increasingly recognised. Bhargava and colleagues showed that the *P. aeruginosa* LasI/RhlI signals 3-oxo-C12-HSL and C4-HSL activate the *lasI* and *rhlI* promoters and modulate *A. baumannii* biofilm in mixed cultures, with reciprocal effects on AHL output [Bhargava 2012]. *P. aeruginosa* exerts a competitive inhibition on *A. baumannii* in co-culture; carbon/QS-based interactions and MvfR (PqsR)-dependent signalling are central, and pharmacological MvfR inhibition partially restores *A. baumannii* survival in co-culture without changing *P. aeruginosa* abundance [Peleg 2008; Wheeler 2025; Cui 2022]. Conversely, indole produced by *A. baumannii* AbiS reduces competitive fitness of *P. aeruginosa* by repressing its QS systems and T3SS, defining a bidirectional signalling axis [Cui 2022; Cui 2023; Cui 2025_2; Li 2025]. Indole derivatives (e.g. 7-hydroxyindole) reduce *abaI*/*abaR* expression and inhibit XDR-AB biofilm, while carnosol disrupts AbiR–promoter binding with a KD of 0.3 μM and reduces biofilm, motility and cytotoxicity by 25–85% in ATCC 17978 [Li 2025; Cui 2025]. Cross-kingdom interactions with *Candida albicans* in airway models facilitate *A. baumannii* pneumonia, plausibly via farnesol/AHL cross-talk, although the molecular interface remains uncharacterised [Tan 2016; Subbarayudu 2024]. Finally, AHL-mediated quorum quenching by environmental and engineered lactonases (MomL, AaL, AidA, PvdQ) reduces inter-species and intra-species signalling in mixed-species biofilms relevant to chronic-wound and ventilator-associated infections [Zhang 2017; Bergonzi 2018; Vogel 2022; Chow 2013; Lpez 2017_2; Mayer 2018; Mayer 2020].

### 3.3.7 Open mechanistic questions and unmapped regulons

Despite intense activity over the past decade, several key questions remain unresolved.

*First*, whether the indole QS system (AbiS synthase / AbiR receptor) operates universally across the species is unclear — most mechanistic work is in ATCC 17978, and the prevalence of functional *abiS*/*abiR* alleles in global IC1/IC2 clones has not been systematically catalogued [Cui 2023; Cui 2025; Cui 2025_2; Djahanschiri 2022]. *Second*, no additional QS signals — equivalents of AI-2, DSF, PQS or anthranilic acid — have yet been firmly established in *A. baumannii*, and whether the species senses heterologous signals via orphan LuxR solos is unknown [Cui 2025_2]. *Third*, c-di-GMP effectors beyond EF-P remain unidentified; the molecular link between c-di-GMP and AbaR-target promoters has not been demonstrated structurally [Cui 2025_2]. *Fourth*, the PmrAB–QS interface, well described in *Salmonella* and *P. aeruginosa* for lipid-A modification and polymyxin resistance, has not been characterised in *A. baumannii*; the same applies to the relationship between QS and the σ-factor RpoN, which is known in *P. aeruginosa* to gate biofilm and motility through *fleQ* and *rhlR* but has no published *A. baumannii* equivalent in the corpus reviewed here. *Fifth*, while LasI/RhlI/PqsR–AbaR cross-talk is documented in vitro, the in vivo significance of polymicrobial AHL/AQ exchange in human infection — including the relative contributions of AHL-degrading host enzymes (paraoxonases) and exogenous QQ — remains an inferential rather than directly demonstrated component of the network [Peleg 2008; Wheeler 2025; Vogel 2022]. *Sixth*, although abaR-binding consensus motifs are emerging from transcriptomic and ChIP-style work, a genome-wide AbaR binding map analogous to LasR ChIP-seq datasets in *P. aeruginosa* has not been published for *A. baumannii* [Sun 2021; Cui 2025_2]. *Seventh*, the role of small regulatory RNAs and nucleoid-associated proteins (AtfA, H-NS) as bridges between QS and antibiotic-resistance regulons is suggested but mechanistically thin [Krger 2016; Escalante 2022]. Addressing these gaps — through cross-clonal mutant libraries, structural AbaR studies, in vivo signal profiling (extending [Carpenito 2025]) and systems-level chemical-biology approaches — should be a priority for the next wave of QS-targeted antivirulence development against carbapenem-resistant *A. baumannii*.
