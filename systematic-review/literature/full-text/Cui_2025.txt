Title: Carnosol attenuates Acinetobacter baumannii virulence by interfering with indole-mediated quorum sensing
PMID: 40660701
PMCID: PMC
DOI: 10.1080/21505594.2025.2530169

=== ABSTRACT ===
# ABSTRACT

Many bacterial pathogens utilize quorum sensing (QS) signals to modulate the biological functions in a cell density-dependent manner. Acinetobacter baumannii is a harmful pathogen and a major cause of hospital-acquired infections due to its severe drug-resistance and pathogenic nature. Therefore, the development of innovative antibacterial strategies for A. baumannii infections is important. Earlier studies have proved that A. baumannii utilizes N-acyl-L-homoserine lactones (AHLs) and indole signalling systems to modulate its biological functions and virulence. Here, we report that carnosol, which is isolated from Salvia officinalis L. (S. officinalis), inhibited the pathogenicity of A. baumannii by interfering with indole-mediated quorum sensing. Phenotypic and virulence experiments have revealed that carnosol decreased the formation of biofilms, motility, and cytotoxicity of A. baumannii, without affecting its growth rate. Genetic and biochemical analysis results showed that carnosol reduced the regulatory effect of AbiR on the transcription of target genes by inhibiting the binding of AbiR to the promoters of these genes. Our results suggest that the indole QS system of A. baumannii could be used as a new target and that carnosol might be developed as a new anti-virulence agent against A. baumannii infections.

# Introduction

As an opportunistic gram-negative bacterium, Acinetobacter baumannii is commonly associated with pneumonia and infections in the bloodstream and urinary tract, resulting in high morbidity and mortality [1]. The prevalence of drug-resistant A. baumannii has notably increased over time, posing significant challenges in clinical management due to the ongoing emergence of infections caused by these strains [2]. The World Health Organization (WHO) has included the carbapenem-resistant A. baumannii in its inaugural inventory of critical antimicrobial-resistant pathogens [3]. Researching and developing new strategies to inhibit the pathogenic phenotype and virulence of A. baumannii while avoiding the generation of antibacterial pressure is important.

The QS signalling systems in bacteria can regulate many biological functions, such as motility, biofilm formation, virulence and drug resistance, making them potential antibacterial targets [4,5]. Earlier studies have demonstrated that the AbaI/AbaR system, a conserved LuxI/LuxR AHL-type QS system in A. baumannii, plays an important role in regulating many biological functions, including biofilm formation, surface motility, and virulence [5–9]. Recent studies revealed that A. baumannii uses another QS signalling system, the indole signalling system, to regulate its biological phenotypes. A. baumannii improves the regulatory activity of AbiR by producing indole, thus controlling its phenotypes and virulence [10,11]. Considering the crucial roles of QS systems in A. baumannii pathogenesis, targeting QS systems for developing new antibiotics could be a potential strategy to combat A. baumannii infections.

Bacterial QS signalling inhibitors derived from natural sources, particularly medicinal plants, have emerged as potential therapeutic agents for the management of bacterial infections [12]. Many traditional Chinese medicine monomers can inhibit bacterial QS [13]. Cajaninstilbene acid and amorfrutins A/B have been identified as Pseudomonas aeruginosa QS inhibitors [14]. Both prodigiosin and glabridin also display the inhibitory activity on A. baumannii QS [15]. In this study, we found that aqueous extracts derived from the leaves of Salvia officinalis L. (S. officinalis), which is a member of the Lamiaceae family and has multiple medicinal activities [16–19], exhibit strong antibacterial activity. Subsequent experiments showed that the isolated carnosol functions as a primary active compound against A. baumannii. This compound can inhibit the biological functions and pathogenicity of A. baumannii by interfering with the indole-mediated QS system. Taken together, our results clarified the mechanism underlying the anti-virulence activity of carnosol against A. baumannii.


# Materials and methods


## Bacterial strains, plasmids, and growth conditions

A list of all strains and plasmids used in this study is included in Table S1. Luria – Bertani (LB) medium (10 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, 5 g/L NaCl; pH 7.0–7.5) or LB agar (LB medium containing 15 g/L agar) was used for culturing A. baumannii and E. coli at 37 °C. Antibiotics, including tetracycline(Tet), kanamycin(Kan), ampicillin(Amp) and amikacin(Amk) were added to the media following specific experimental protocols. The chromogenic substrate 5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl-β-D-galactopyranoside (X-gal) was used at a concentration of 20 mg/mL. S. officinalis was obtained from Zhejiang Province, China.


## Phenotypic analysis

Motility was measured on semisolid agar [20]. The 1% tryptone, 0.5% NaCl and 0.3% agar were combined to form semisolid agar plates. The bacteria were injected into the centres of semisolid agar plates containing a series of concentrations of carnosol. After 8 h of incubation at 37 °C, the diameter of the movement trace was measured.

We evaluated biofilm formation using 96-well polystyrene plates as previously described, but with a few modifications [21]. The overnight cultures of A. baumannii were adjusted to an optical density at 600 nm (OD600) of 0.1 with LB medium. Then, 100 μL aliquots of the bacterial cultures were added to 96-well polystyrene plates containing different concentrations of carnosol. Following a 24-hour incubation period at 37 °C, the OD600 was measured for cell growth, and any non-adherent cells were eliminated from the wells. The wells were rinsed with sterile phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) and subsequently treated with a 0.1% solution of crystal violet for a duration of 15 minutes. Subsequently, the wells were rinsed multiple times with ddH2O prior to the addition of 95% ethanol. The OD590 of the resulting solution was assessed to quantify the extent of biofilm formation.

Cytotoxicity was evaluated by quantifying the lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) from A549 cells using established methods [11]. A549 cells were cultured in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle’s medium [DMEM; 10% foetal bovine serum (FBS)] in 96-well culture plates at 1.0 × 105 cells per well. The overnight-cultured bacteria were centrifuged and resuspended in DMEM (add 1% FBS) to an OD600 = 0.1. A549 cells were infected with A. baumannii at 109 CFU/mL for 8 h. Introducing different concentrations of carnosol into the culture system for experimental treatment. A CytoTOX96 kit (Promega, Madison, WI) was used to measure the release of LDH.


## Molecular docking analysis

In FASTA format, the AbiR protein sequence (accession no: ABO11822.2) was downloaded from the GenBank database. SWISS-MODEL was used to construct the 3D model of AbiR (http://swissmodel. ExPASy.org) [22]. We selected the highest quality templates for building the models. PDB files containing the predicted model output were downloaded for further analysis. To investigate the binding mode of carnosol in the 3D model of AbiR, docking studies were performed using AUTODOCK tools 1.5 [23] and the binding interaction between carnosol and AbiR was visualized by the PyMol package [24].


## Mutagenesis of AbiR by site-directed mutagenesis

Four residues were mutated to Ala. The genome of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 was employed as an amplification template. For the construction of substitution mutants of target genes, we used an overlapping PCR mutagenesis method. The OMEGA Plasmid Mini Kit (USA) was used for plasmid purification. The DNA sequencing process was used to confirm all mutations. The verified plasmids harbouring the desired mutations were introduced into the E. coli BL21 (DE3) strain, followed by the purification of mutant proteins using a protocol identical to that used for wild-type proteins.


## Microscale thermophoresis (MST)

A Monolith NT.115 instrument was used for protein-binding experiments (NanoTemper Technologie). The AbiR protein was labelled using the L001 Monolith NT.115 Protein Labelling Kit (Nano Temper, Munich, Germany). Subsequently, the labelled AbiR and different concentrations of unlabelled carnosol were added to standard treated silicon capillaries (K002 Monolith NT.115), and fluorescence measurements were obtained.


## Electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA)

The EMSA experiment was performed as previously described, but with a few modifications [25]. DNA probes for EMSA were amplified by PCR and purified on agarose gels. In a 10 μL reaction system, 30 ng of DNA probes were mixed with tag-free proteins in a Tris buffer (20 mM Tris- HCl,4 mM MgCl2,100 mM NaCl,1 mM dithiothreitol, 1% NP-40 and 10% glycerol, pH 7.4). When needed, different concentrations of carnosol were added to the above reaction system. After incubation for 20 min at room temperature, the reaction mixtures were subjected to 5% native polyacrylamide gel and run in the 0.5 × TBE buffer at 100 V. The DNA probe was stained using red nucleic acid gel stain (MCE) and imaged by the Tanon gel analysis software (Tanon 1600, China). Uncropped images of gels can be found in the original data file.


## Mouse systemic infection model

This experiment was performed using 6–8 weeks BALB/c mice and was conducted according to the National Institutes of Health Guide for the Care and Use of Laboratory Animals (NIH publication no. 8023, revised 1978). The animal experiments for this project were approved by the ethics committee of School of Pharmaceutical Sciences (Shenzhen), Sun Yat-sen University (SYSU-YXYSZ20230814). All mice were allowed free access to food and water. After 7 days of adaptive feeding, forty BALB/c mice between the ages of 6 and 8 weeks were randomly divided into four groups (n = 10). For the mouse mortality model [11], the mice were intraperitoneally injected with 100 μL bacterial cells (OD600 = 0.8). After 4 h post inoculation, carnosol (100 μM) was orally administered to the mice for treatment. The control group was treated with the same protocol but injected only with 100 μL PBS buffer. The mortalities were assessed during the initial 7-day period following infection.

At the end of the experiment, the remaining mice were humanely euthanized using 100% carbon dioxide gas delivered at a flow rate of 20–30% of chamber volume per minute, with continuous exposure until complete loss of consciousness. This was followed by cervical dislocation to ensure death, performed after confirming the absence of corneal reflexes and pedal withdrawal responses. The procedure adhered to the AVMA (American Veterinary Medical Association) Guidelines on Euthanasia in 2020.

In this study, the design, execution, and reporting of the animal experiments were strictly adhered to the principles outlined in the ARRIVE guidelines (Animal Research: Reporting of In Vivo Experiments), to ensure transparency, reproducibility, and ethical conduct of the experiments.


## Statistical analysis

The sample size was not predetermined using a statistical method. There were no data excluded from the analysis. The data were presented as the mean ± standard deviation (SD) from three independent experiments. Statistical analyses were performed using Prism 9 software (GraphPad). The statistical analysis employed in this study involved the utilization of the Student’s t-test to compare data from two distinct groups and one-way ANOVA for comparisons involving more than two groups. p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant. *p < 0.05; **p < 0.01; ***p < 0.001; ns = not significant. Biological replicates and numbers of independent experiments are provided in the legends. All of the experiments presented as representative gels were repeated at least three times with similar results.


# Results


## The extract of S. officinalis inhibits the virulence of A. baumannii

The greatest challenge in treating A. baumannii infections in clinical practice is its high level of drug resistance. Therefore, novel antibiotics that can overcome drug resistance are urgently needed [26]. S. officinalis is widely recognized for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties [27]. We first investigated the effects of S. officinalis extract on A. baumannii ATCC 17978 phenotypes, including motility, biofilm formation, and cytotoxicity. After soaking for 24 h with 75% ethanol, 2.4 kg of original S. officinalis medicinal material was extracted and 43.2 g crude extract was obtained. Then, 100 mg extract was dissolved in 10 mL methanol and the 1% (0.1 mg/mL) extract of S. officinalis was found to significantly decrease the motility and biofilm formation of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 by 75.0% and 55.8%, respectively. Furthermore, the extract notably attenuated the cytotoxicity of A. baumannii to A549 cells, with a 66.5% reduction observed at 8 h post-inoculation (Figure 1a-c). Meanwhile, the exogenous addition of 1% extract of S. officinalis showed no effect on the growth rate of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 in LB (Figure S1).
Figure 1.Influence of S. officinalis extract on the phenotypes and virulence of A. baumannii. Effect of 1% S. officinalis extract (0.1 mg/mL) on the motility (a), biofilm formation (b) and cytotoxicity (c) of A. baumannii. The data are presented as the means ± standard deviations of three independent experiments. The significance was determined by a two-sided Student’s t-test, (***p < 0.001).


## Carnosol is the primary active component of S. officinalis

To investigate the active constituents of the S. officinalis extract that inhibit the physiology and pathogenesis of A. baumannii, we employed HPLC to assess the active fractions from the S. officinalis with different polarities (Figure 2a). Among these fractions, 10 μg/mL of the peak with a retention time of 28.2 min exhibited significant inhibitory effects on the motility, biofilm formation, and cytotoxicity of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 by 93.14%, 84.76% and 84.53%, respectively. LC-MS analysis of the purified peak revealed a molecular ion [M+H]+ with a m/z ratio of 331.4 (Figure 2b), which in concert with the molecular formula C20H26O4. The 1H NMR spectrum and the 13C NMR data of the compound shown in Figure S2a, b suggest that the active compound is carnosol.

We then determined the effects of different concentrations of carnosol on the inhibitory activity of A. baumannii and found that 20 µM carnosol suppressed the motility, biofilm formation, and cytotoxicity of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 by 48.21%, 24.60% and 26.48%, respectively (Figure 2c–e). To confirm whether carnosol has bactericidal effects, we investigated the effect of carnosol on the growth rate of A. baumannii ATCC 17978. The growth curve results revealed that the exogenous addition of 100 µM carnosol had little impact on the growth rate of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 in LB (Figure S3). These results suggest that carnosol treatment effectively inhibited the pathogenic phenotype and virulence of A. baumannii. However, carnosol did not exert selective pressure on the growth of bacterial cells. Moreover, the compound also exhibits non-toxic properties towards the A549 cell line (Figure S4). We further investigated the effect of carnosol on the QS systems of A. baumannii and found that the exogenous addition of 100 µM carnosol had no impact on the transcription of both the signal synthase-encoding genes and regulator-encoding genes of the AHL and indole signalling systems (Figure 2f,g).


## Carnosol downregulates the expression of many genes in A. baumannii

RNA sequencing (RNA-Seq) was used to analyse and compare the transcriptomes of A. baumannii wild-type strains in the absence or presence of carnosol. Differential gene expression analysis revealed that 76 genes were upregulated, whereas 159 genes were downregulated in A. baumannii ATCC 17978 in the presence of 100 μM carnosol (Log2 fold-change ≥1.0) (Figure 3a). It was found that these genes were involved in a wide range of biological processes, such as translation, transcription, secondary metabolites biosynthesis, energy production and conversion, defence, and coenzyme metabolism (Figure 3b, Table S3). RT-qPCR analysis of selected genes confirmed the RNA-seq results. Interestingly, we found that the differential expression of some target genes with the exogenous addition of carnosol was also controlled by AbiR, suggesting that carnosol might interact with the indole QS signalling system (Figure 3c).

## Carnosol controls the transcription of AbiR-regulated target genes in A. baumannii by directly binding to AbiR

As carnosol did not affect the expression level of genes involved in the indole signalling but controlled the expression of some target genes regulated by AbiR, we further studied the interaction between carnosol and AbiR. We purified the AbiR protein and investigated whether carnosol can bind to it. MST experiment results revealed that AbiR binds to carnosol with an estimated dissociation constant (KD) of 0.3±0.13 µM (Figure 4a,b). We also purified the other QS regulator, AbaR, and investigated whether carnosol could bind to it. MST analysis revealed that AbaR was not able to bind to carnosol (Figure S5a, b). In our previous study, we demonstrated that AbiR, which is a receptor protein of indole, regulates the expression of the csuA/BABCDE cluster and pilW by directly binding to their promoters[11]. RT–qPCR and the promoter-lacZ fusion reporter assay results revealed that carnosol downregulated the expression of the csuA/BABCDE cluster and pilW (Figure 4c–e), which are crucial for bacterial virulence, adhesion, and biofilm formation [11,28,29]. We then used these two target genes to study the mechanism by which carnosol treatment affects the regulatory activity of AbiR in detail. Further EMSA results shown in Figure 4f,g, the binding of AbiR to the promoter probes of the csuA/BABCDE operon and pilW was inhibited following administration of the carnosol reaction mixtures. Furthermore, we also found that indole can reverse the inhibitory activity of carnosol on AbiR (Figure S6). We then endeavored to ascertain the specific-binding sites of carnosol on AbiR. Autodocking analysis showed four amino acid residues, Tyr209 (Y209), His266 (H266), lle236 (I236) and Trp267 (W267) that might be critical for the interaction between AbiR and carnosol (Figure 4h). We then purified AbiRY209A, AbiRH266A, AbiRI236A, and AbiRW267A protein. MST analysis result revealed that mutations at Y209 not only significantly weakened the binding between AbiR and carnosol (Figure 4i–l) but also eliminated the impact of carnosol on the binding of AbiR to the DNA promoter of the target gene (Figure 4m).

## Carnosol attenuates pathogenesis and suppresses inflammation caused by A. baumannii

The indole signalling system plays a crucial role in the pathogenesis of A.
baumannii [11]. Because carnosol can inhibit the cytotoxicity of A. baumannii and interfere with AbiR regulatory activity, we further investigated the effect of carnosol on the pathogenesis of A. baumannii by using mouse infection model. The mortalities of mice infected with the A. baumannii ATCC 17978 in the absence or presence of 100 µM carnosol at 4 days post-infection were 80% and 30%, respectively (Figure 5a).

Earlier studies have demonstrated that A. baumannii activates the NLRP3/Caspase1 pathway to cause inflammatory damage [30]. Carnosol possesses a phenolic structural moiety and has the ability to reduce cellular reactive oxygen species (ROS) by either directly scavenging them or indirectly by enhancing antioxidant defences [31]. Along with these activities, multiple biological effects and therapeutic potential are orchestrated via the NLRP3 inflammasome [31]. Subsequently, we determined whether carnosol can inhibit A. baumannii-induced inflammasome activation using the RAW 264.7 cell line. As shown in Figure 5b–g, A. baumannii induced the expression of many inflammatory factors, such as caspase-1, ASC, NLRP3, IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α, whereas the exogenous addition of carnosol effectively inhibited the expression of these proinflammatory factors.


## The synergistic effect of carnosol and antibiotics on clinical drug-resistant A. baumannii strains

Owing to the excellent inhibitory effect of carnosol on the biofilm formation of A. baumannii, we determined whether carnosol has a synergistic effect with antibiotic drugs on drug-resistant A. baumannii. Minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) experiments showed that 128 µg/mL carnosol did not inhibit the growth of A. baumannii ATCC 17978 but increased its sensitivity to ampicillin and amikacin (Table 1). We also collected ampicillin and aminoglycoside-resistant A. baumannii strains from Ningbo Medical Center Lihuili Hospital to investigate the synergistic effect of carnosol with antibiotics. Consistent with the results obtained for the wild-type strain of A. baumannii ATCC 17978, the carnosol also enhanced the sensitivity of clinical strains to ampicillin and amikacin (Table 2). Further experimental analysis revealed that 32 µg/mL amikacin cannot inhibit biofilm formation and cytotoxicity in the aminoglycoside-resistant strain. The addition of 20 µM carnosol decreased the biofilm formation and the cytotoxicity of this strain by 30.60% and 34.50%, respectively (Figure 6a,b). Intriguingly, the combination of 20 µM carnosol with 32 µg/mL amikacin decreased the biofilm formation and cytotoxicity of this strain by 62.80% and 67.18%, respectively (Figure 6a,b), indicating the excellent synergistic effect of carnosol with ampicillin and amikacin on the clinical drug-resistant A. baumannii strain; this result also supports the potential use of carnosol as an antibacterial drug.

# Discussion

A. baumannii, a prevalent bacterium responsible for nosocomial infections, presents a significant clinical challenge owing to its high prevalence of multidrug resistance (MDRAB) [3,32]. Traditional antibiotics, including cephalosporins, penicillin, tetracycline, quinolones, and aminoglycosides, have frequently showed ineffectiveness to A. baumannii due to the drug resistance of this bacterium [1,26,33]. The urgent need to fight A. baumannii makes searching for new antimicrobial agents necessary. Several studies have reported the efficacy of naturally derived compounds as anti-biofilm and anti-virulence agents against A. baumannii [34]. α-Mangostin exerts its anti-biofilm effects by interrupting initial biofilm formation and the cell-to-cell signalling mechanism of A. baumannii [35]. The antimicrobial agent curcumin inhibited the pellicle formation and surface motility of A. baumannii [36]. In this study, we discovered that carnosol reduced the virulence and QS-related bacterial phenotypes of A. baumannii but had little effect on its growth (Figures 2c–e and S3). More meaningfully, the carnosol also exhibits good synergistic effects with amikacin and ampicillin in the treatment of these resistant strains (Figure 6a,b, Tables 1 and 2). This result is consistent with the result of a previous study on the improvement of aminoglycoside antibacterial activity by carnosol [37]. Our research suggested that carnosol has potential as a drug for further research and development, particularly for treating drug-resistant A. baumannii.

QS is a process by which a large variety of bacteria produce and detect signalling molecules and thereby coordinate their biological behaviours in a cell density-dependent manner [38–43]. The development of QS inhibitors is important for inhibiting drug resistance, biofilm formation, motility and virulence factor release in bacteria [44–46]. Flavonoids from Glycyrrhiza glabra were found to reduce the virulence of A. baumannii by decreasing the activity of the QS system [47]. Oridonin attenuates Burkholderia cenocepacia virulence by suppressing QS signalling [48]. As a structural analog of the cis-2-dodecenoic acid signal, 14-Me-C16:Δ2 inhibited the virulence of B. cenocepacia by disrupting its QS system [49]. In A. baumannii, indole signalling system regulates the virulence and pathogenic phenotypes through the regulator protein AbiR, a potential antibacterial target [11]. The RNA-seq results showed that the differential expression levels of some genes after the addition of exogenous carnosol were consistent with those in the abiR deletion mutant (Figure 3a–c). Thus, we speculated that AbiR may serve as a potential target for the carnosol. Further experimental analysis revealed that carnosol exhibited a direct binding affinity towards AbiR, thus affecting AbiR-regulated gene expression in A. baumannii (Figure 4a–g). The aforementioned findings suggest that specifically inhibiting the indole QS signalling in A. baumannii serves as an effective approach for combating infections.

S. officinalis, a member of the Lamiaceae family, has been explored for the treatment of fever and sweating, rheumatism, bronchitis, and mental and nervous disorders [16–19]. In this study, we assessed the potential of S. officinalis, a natural medicinal plant traditionally used for treating bacterial infections [16,17]. We found that S. Officinalis extract attenuated the QS-related bacterial phenotypes and cytotoxicity of A. baumannii (Figure 1a–c). Further experimental analysis revealed that the key active ingredient in the S. officinalis extract is carnosol (Figures 2a,b and S2). Carnosol derived from S. officinalis is a polyphenol, which exhibited antioxidant properties, anti-inflammatory, neuroprotective, cell protective effects, antibacterial activity and anticancer effects [37,50–55]. The antimicrobial properties of carnosol have garnered immense attention recently [54,56,57]. However, the precise mechanisms and specific targets underlying its antibacterial effects remain elusive. In this study, we clarified that the carnosol may exert inhibitory effects on the transcriptional regulatory activity of AbiR by changing the 3D conformation of AbiR (Figure 4h–m). Our previous research found that the homologous proteins of AbiR exist in various bacteria [11]. Therefore, carnosol is promising for further development as a broad-spectrum antibacterial agent.

Previous studies have shown that A. baumannii strains can induce proinflammatory cytokine release and programmed cell death in mice, leading to an inflammatory response [58]. Consistent with previous reports, we found that after infection with A. baumannii, the expression of many inflammatory factors, such as caspase-1, ASC, NLRP3, IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α, in the RAW264.7 cell line, significantly increased. Moreover, exogenous addition of carnosol caused significant inhibition of proinflammatory factor expression in the RAW264.7 cell line following infection with A. baumannii (Figure 5b–g). Besides to be a universal QS signal, indole plays a crucial role in mediating the interaction between gut bacteria and hosts by activating the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR) [59–61]. In summary, this study provides a theoretical basis for the development of natural antimicrobial medicinal products for the treatment of chronic infections and inflammation caused by pathogens with the AbiS/AbiR indole signalling system.
