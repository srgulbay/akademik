Title: A response regulator controls Acinetobacter baumannii virulence by acting as an indole receptor
PMID: 37649583
PMCID: PMC
DOI: 10.1093/pnasnexus/pgad274

=== ABSTRACT ===
# Abstract

Indole is an important signal employed by many bacteria to modulate intraspecies signaling and interspecies or interkingdom communication. Our recent study revealed that indole plays a key role in regulating the physiology and virulence of Acinetobacter baumannii. However, it is not clear how A. baumannii perceives and responds to the indole signal in modulating biological functions. Here, we report that indole controls the physiology and virulence of A. baumannii through a previously uncharacterized response regulator designated as AbiR (A1S_1394), which contains a cheY-homologous receiver (REC) domain and a helix-turn-helix (HTH) DNA-binding domain. AbiR controls the same biological functions as the indole signal, and indole-deficient mutant phenotypes were rescued by in trans expression of AbiR. Intriguingly, unlike other response regulators that commonly interact with signal ligands through the REC domain, AbiR binds to indole with a high affinity via an unusual binding region, which is located between its REC and HTH domains. This interaction substantially enhances the activity of AbiR in promoter binding and in modulation of target gene expression. Taken together, our results present a widely conserved regulator that controls bacterial physiology and virulence by sensing the indole signal in a unique mechanism.

=== FULL TEXT ===
# Significance Statement

Acinetobacter baumannii is an important opportunistic human pathogen. Following our previous finding that A. baumannii employs indole as a key quorum sensing (QS) signal in the regulation of its virulence, here, we demonstrate that indole signaling depends on a previously uncharacterized response regulator AbiR. The significance of this study lies in the identification of AbiR as the indole receptor in A. baumannii and hence unveiling a key component in the indole-mediated QS signaling pathway. In addition, this study adds AbiR as a member to the list of indole effectors, which together with the indole synthase AbiS, are present in diverse bacterial species, suggesting that the indole-based AbiS/AbiR signaling system could be a widely conserved QS system.


# Introduction

Quorum sensing (QS) is a process by which bacteria produce and detect signal molecules and thereby coordinate their biological behaviors in a cell density–dependent manner (1–3). Multiple QS signaling molecules, such as N-acyl-l-homoserine lactones (AHLs), diffusible signal factor family signals, autoinducer-2, 2-heptyl-3-hydroxy-4(1H)-quinolone, 2-heptyl-4-quinolone, methyl 3-hydroxypalmitate, autoinducer-3, bradyoxetin, and diketopiperazines, have been identified (4–13). Anthranilic acid was also recently reported as a signal that regulates the physiology and pathogenicity of Ralstonia solanacearum through the receptor RaaR (14, 15). As an important cell-to-cell communication signal, indole was found to modulate diverse biological processes, including motility, biofilm formation, and drug resistance, through intraspecies signaling and interspecies or interkingdom communication (16–26).

Acinetobacter baumannii is a common nosocomial infection–causing bacterium, and the prevalence of multidrug-resistant A. baumannii poses a great challenge in clinical treatment (27–29). Previous studies have shown that the QS system of A. baumannii is composed mainly of AbaI and AbaR, which are homologous to the typical LuxI/LuxR system found in other gram-negative bacteria (30–32). AbaI functions as an autoinducer synthase, producing AHL signal molecules with different long carbon acyl side chains, the major one of which is 3-OH-C12-HSL (32–34). The LuxR family transcriptional regulator AbaR has a conserved helix-turn-helix (HTH)-LuxR domain. AbaR regulates biological functions such as biofilm formation, surface motility, and virulence by binding to AHL signal molecules (32, 35, 36).

Our recent study showed that indole is an important signal utilized by A. baumannii cells to control physiology and virulence. The biosynthesis of indole is performed by AbiS in A. baumannii (26). Deletion of abiS led to impaired phenotypes, including biofilm formation, motility activity, and virulence, in A. baumannii (26). We also found that indole from A. baumannii reduced the competitive fitness of Pseudomonas aeruginosa by inhibiting its QS systems and type III secretion system (26). In this study, we identified a receptor responsive to indole, AbiR (A. baumannii indole regulator), which is composed of an cheY-homologous receiver (REC) domain and an HTH domain. Interestingly, there is an indole-binding motif located between the REC domain and the HTH domain. Perception of indole enhances the binding of AbiR to the promoters of target genes, leading to the modulation of gene expression levels. We also found that homologs of the regulator AbiR are present in many other bacteria. These findings not only shed new light on the mechanism of indole signaling in A. baumannii but may also present a potent therapeutical target for the control of this important bacterial pathogen.


# Results


## AbiR controls indole-regulated phenotypes in A. baumannii

The csuA/BABCDE operon is controlled by the indole signal (Fig. S1) (26). To identify the regulatory components of the indole signaling system, we screened a Tn5 mutant library of A. baumannii ATCC17978 carrying a csuA/BABCDE operon-lacZ promoter fusion plasmid for colonies that were light blue on LB agar plates supplemented with X-gal (37–39). We screened ∼30,000 colonies and identified mutants, including those with insertions in the known cyclic di-GMP (c-di-GMP) synthase-encoding gene A1S_2506, which is involved in c-di-GMP metabolism and csuA/BABCDE expression (Table S1) (40). In addition, we identified a gene annotated as a transcriptional response regulator (A1S_1394), which consisted of an REC domain and an HTH domain (Fig. 1a and b). Moreover, we found that in trans expression of A1S_1394 partially restored all the tested phenotypes of ΔabiS to the wild-type strain levels, including motility, biofilm formation, and cytotoxicity, which are all regulated by the indole signaling system (Fig. 1c–e). As A1S_1394 has a DNA-binding domain and a signal receiver domain, we hypothesized that it might be a downstream regulator of the indole signaling system and thus named it AbiR.

## AbiR is a key downstream regulator of the indole signaling system

As in trans expression of AbiR partially restored the defective phenotypes of the indole-deficient mutant ΔabiS, we then continued to investigate the role of AbiR in regulating these important biological functions. We found that deletion of abiR caused the same phenotypic changes as those observed for the indole-deficient mutant ΔabiS but did not affect the growth rate of the bacterial cells in LB or MP medium (Fig. S2a and b). Compared with the A. baumannii wild-type strain, the abiR deletion mutant exhibited decreased motility and biofilm formation by 36.91 and 41.34%, respectively, and attenuated cytotoxicity in human A549 cells by 52.37% (Fig. 2a–c).

Moreover, deletion of both abiS and abiR caused a decrease in motility, biofilm formation, and cytotoxicity by 59.39, 45.66, and 55.15%, respectively (Fig. 2a–c) but resulted in only a slight decrease in the growth rate (Fig. S3a and b). Interestingly, we found that in trans expression of abiR partially complemented the phenotypes of ΔabiS, while the addition of exogenous indole did not restore these biological functions in either the abiR deletion mutant or the abiS and abiR double-deletion mutant to wild-type strain levels (Fig. 2a–c), suggesting that AbiR is a key downstream regulator of the indole signaling system in A. baumannii.

Further investigation was conducted to study the detailed role of AbiR in the pathogenesis of A. baumannii. It was found that the mortalities of mice infected with the wild type, ΔabiR, ΔabiR(abiR), ΔabiS, ΔabiSΔabiR, and ΔabiS(abiS) at 3 days post infection were 70, 35, 70, 20, 20, and 80%, respectively (Fig. 2d). Consistently, deletion of abiR obviously reduced lung injury of mice infected by A. baumannii (Fig. 2e and f).


## AbiR regulates target gene expression by directly binding to the promoter

To further confirm that AbiR is a key downstream regulator of the indole signaling system and elucidate the regulatory mechanism, we employed chromatin immunoprecipitation (a ChIP-Seq assay) to identify the target genes that were directly regulated by AbiR (Table S2). To determine whether transcriptional regulation of the selected genes is achieved by direct binding of AbiR to their promoters, AbiR, which has 319 amino acids and a calculated molecular weight of 35.82 kDa, was purified using affinity chromatography and prepared for electrophoretic mobility shift assays (EMSAs; Fig. 3a). Among the potential target genes, both the csuA/BABCDE cluster and the pilW are associated with the biological functions and the pathogenicity of A. baumannii (37, 38). The RT‒qPCR analysis and the promoter-lacZ fusion reporter assay results showed that both the csuA/BABCDE system and the pilW were downregulated in the abiR mutant strain, so the two target genes were chosen for further study (Fig. 3b–d). A PCR-amplified 270-bp DNA fragment from the csuA/BABCDE promoter and a PCR-amplified 356-bp DNA fragment from the pilW promoter were used as probes. The EMSAs confirmed the binding of AbiR to the csuA/BABCDE and pilW promoter DNA fragments, and the amounts of probes bound to AbiR increased with increasing amounts of AbiR (Fig. 3e and f). In addition, we purified the HTH domain of AbiR and found that the amounts of labeled probes bound to the HTH domain increased with increasing amounts of the HTH domain, suggesting that the HTH domain binds to the promoters of target genes (Fig. 3g–i). We also purified the REC domain of AbiR and found that it could not bound to the promoter of csuA/BABCDE (Fig. S4).

Sequence reads were obtained from two independent ChIP-Seq tests using a FLAG-specific antibody and mapped to the A. baumannii ATCC17978 genome. The ChIP-Seq data revealed that AbiR bound various gene promoters (Table S2). HOMER (version 3) analysis of the ChIP-Seq data revealed that those promoters contained a potential AbiR-binding motif (Fig. 3j). The potential binding sites 5′-ATAAGATT-3′and 5′-ATGCCTAT-3′were then deleted from the promoter regions of csuA/BABCDE and pilW, respectively (Fig. 3j). EMSA analysis showed that no DNA‒protein complex was formed when the binding sites were deleted from the probes (Fig. 3k and l), suggesting that the fragments are essential for the binding of AbiR to the promoters of csuA/BABCDE and pilW.

To further examine the binding sites, some additional target genes were chosen for EMSA analysis. The amounts of labeled probes of A1S_0048, A1S_0655, and A1S_3138 bound to AbiR also increased with increasing amounts of AbiR but decreased in the presence of a 50-fold higher concentration of the unlabeled probe (Fig. S5a–c). Deletion of the potential binding sequences, 5′-ATGCATAT-3′, 5′-ATCCGTAT-3′, and 5′-ATGCATAT-3′, from the promoter regions of A1S_0048, A1S_0655, and A1S_3138, respectively, abolished their binding to AbiR (Fig. S5d). Together, these results demonstrated that AbiR regulates target gene expression by directly binding to a specific region in the gene promoters.


## Indole is a signal ligand of AbiR

Since AbiR controls the same biological functions as the indole signal, we initially speculated that indole might control the expression of abiR. Therefore, we measured the expression of abiR in ΔabiS using a β-galactosidase activity assay. As shown in Fig. S6, the transcription level of abiR was not affected by deletion of abiS. Given that AbiR consisted of an REC domain and an HTH domain, we hypothesized that AbiR may modulate biological functions by interacting with indole. To confirm our hypothesis, we performed microscale thermophoresis (MST) assays to test whether AbiR could bind to indole. As shown in Fig. 4a, MST analysis showed that AbiR bound tightly to indole, with an estimated dissociation constant (KD) of 5.9 ± 1.30 μM. We also tested the molecular interaction between AbiR and the indole analogs 2-(2,2-di (1H-indole-3-yl) ethyl) aniline (di-IEA) and methyl 2-(1H-indole-3-yl) acetate (MIA) and found that there was no binding between AbiR and the two compounds (Fig. S7), suggesting that the binding between AbiR and indole is specific. In addition, we purified a polypeptide containing only the REC domain (Fig. 4b) or HTH domain of AbiR and tested them for indole binding. MST analysis revealed that indole did not interact with the REC domain or HTH domain (Fig. 4c and d).

## Indole binds to a specific region in AbiR

We continued to attempt to identify the indole-binding motif of AbiR. Autodocking analysis predicted four amino acid residues, Tyr209 (Y209), Leu235 (L235), lle236 (I236), and His266 (H266), that might be critical for the interaction between AbiR and indole (Fig. 5a). To confirm the indole-binding region (IBR), we purified the protein fragment of AbiR from K131 to L270, with a molecular weight of 15.92 kDa (Fig. 5b and c). The MST results showed that there was a strong interaction between the new IBR and indole, with an estimated dissociation constant (KD) of 12.1 ± 3.17 μM (Fig. 5d), suggesting that it is not the REC domain, but this specific region with 139 aa between the REC and HTH domain, which was named the IBR, that is responsible for the perception of indole.

M: marker. d) MST analysis of the binding of indole to the indole-binding region fragment. e) SDS‒PAGE of the purified AbiRY209A, AbiRL235A, AbiRI236A, and AbiRH266A proteins. MST analysis of the binding of indole to f) AbiRY209A, g) AbiRH266A, h) AbiRL235A, and i) AbiRI236A. The data are the means ± SD from three independent experiments. Error bars indicate SD.]

We further studied the function of the IBR, which is located between the REC domain and HTH_LuxR domain (Fig. 5b). We then generated four single-point mutant proteins (AbiRY209A, AbiRL235A, AbiRI236A, and AbiRH266A; Fig. 5e). MST analysis showed that mutations at Y209 and H266 not only markedly weakened the binding between AbiR and indole (Fig. 5f–i) but also partially impaired the function of AbiR (Fig. S8).

As indole is widely employed by bacterial cells, we investigated whether AbiR homologs of other bacterial species, including Acinetobacter pittii, Burkholderia cenocepacia, and Halothiobacillus neapolitanus, which share 99.37, 46.90, and 47.33% identity with AbiR, respectively, bind to indole. Among them, we found that the homolog of AbiR from A. pittii has the same key binding amino acid sites as AbiR from A. baumannii. We purified these three homologous proteins (Fig. 6a), and the binding affinities of these proteins with indole were tested by MST. Intriguingly, we found that the potential homologs of AbiR from B. cenocepacia and H. neapolitanus, which do not have the key amino acid residues Y209 and H266, did not bind to indole, but the homolog of AbiR from A. pittii bound to indole with an estimated dissociation constant (KD) of 10.9  ± 1.47 μM (Fig. 6b–d). Furthermore, BLAST search results of the AbiS/AbiR/IBR homologs revealed that the indole signaling system might be widely present in bacteria (Table S3).

## Indole enhances the binding of AbiR to target promoter DNA

To determine how the binding of indole to AbiR might affect the activity of AbiR, we then tested the effects of indole on the binding of AbiR to the csuA/BABCDE and pilW promoters by EMSA. As shown in Fig. 7a and b, the binding of AbiR to the csuA/BABCDE and pilW promoter probes was enhanced when indole was present, and the binding activity of AbiR increased with increasing amounts of indole. Furthermore, mutation of the indole-binding sites (Y209A and H266A) eliminated the effect of indole on the binding ability of AbiR to the target promoter DNA (Fig. 7c–f). Together, these results suggest that the addition of indole enhanced the activity of AbiR to control target gene expression.

## AbiR controls the expression of a wide range of genes in A. baumannii

To further study the regulatory roles of AbiR in controlling bacterial physiology, we analyzed and compared the transcriptomes of the A. baumannii wild-type strain and the ΔabiR mutant by using RNA sequencing (RNA-Seq). Differential gene expression analysis showed that 29 genes were upregulated and 84 genes were downregulated in the ΔabiR mutant compared with the wild-type strain (Log2 fold-change ≥1.0; Table S4). These genes were associated with a range of biological functions, including transcription, translation, lipid metabolism, signal transduction, and energy production and conversion (Table S4 and Fig. S9).


# Discussion

Indole is widely utilized by bacterial cells to modulate intraspecies signaling and interspecies or interkingdom communication in microbial communities. It was reported that many kinds of gram-negative and gram-positive bacteria can produce indole, and both indole-producing bacteria and nonindole-producing bacteria could coordinate their behaviors in response to extracellular indole (16, 17). Indole has received much attention because of its important roles, including modulating microbial biofilm formation, bacterial pathogenesis, plasmid stabilization, spore formation, and acid resistance; it can also cause stress responses of host cells, including an increase in tight junction resistance in epithelial cells and a decrease in the levels of inflammatory biomarkers in host cells (16–26, 41–46). In this study, we identified a receptor protein of indole, which was named AbiR (Fig. 1a). AbiR regulates the cellular functions of A. baumannii, including motility, biofilm formation and virulence (Fig. 2). An unexpected finding is that our previous result showed that A. baumannii utilizes indole to modulate the AHL QS system in A. baumannii. Mutation of abiS caused a decrease in both the expression level of abaI and the production of AHL signal (26). However, in this study, we found that there was no obvious change in the expression of abaI and the production of 3-OH-C12-HSL in the abiR mutant (Fig. S10a–c). In addition, EMSA analysis showed that there was no protein-DNA binding between AbiR and abaI promoter (Fig. S10d), suggesting that AbiR is not the sole receptor of indole signal in A. baumannii. Together, our results not only provide additional evidence of the important role of indole in intraspecies signaling but also reveal a new indole signaling system composed of the indole synthase AbiS and the indole-responsive regulator AbiR.

There are several verified or potential indole effectors, which could be clearly classified into two categories based on cell of origin. The first category consists of some sensors, regulators, and enzymes in bacteria, including the two-component systems BaeSR and CpxAR in Escherichia coli (19), Le-QseC in Lysobacter enzymogenes (20), EmhR in Pseudomonas fluorescens (21), pyruvate kinase in Stigmatella aurantiaca (47), the RNA polymerase regulator protein DksA in Vibrio cholerae (23), and IsrR in enterohemorrhagic E. coli (48). The second category is the aryl hydrocarbon receptor (AHR) of host cells. Indole can activate AHR to induce IL-22, which promotes stem cell–mediated repair of the epithelial barrier and protects against infection and damage caused by hyperinflammatory responses (49–52). However, the A. baumannii indole signaling system is distinctive in two aspects. First, as indicated previously, AbiS produces indole using a different biosynthetic pathway from that of the indole synthase tryptophanase (TnaA) (26). Second, the responsive regulator AbiR is distinct, as it contains a specific IBR located between the REC domain and HTH_LuxR domain in AbiR (Fig. 5). There is another LuxR-type regulator, SdiA, which contains an autoinducer-binding domain and a LuxR family domain and is involved in indole signaling in E. coli (22, 53, 54). However, there is no evidence of the direct binding of indole to SdiA thus far (16). Our MST assays also revealed that there is no direct interaction between SdiA and indole (Fig. S11). Together, these findings suggest that AbiR is a unique receptor for indole, whose significance is worth exploring in the future.

Bioinformatics analysis showed that A1S_1393 and AbiR (A1S_1394) may form a two-component system. REC domains are thought to be small-molecule signal-receiving domains and function as phosphorylation-regulated switches to control the activities of effector domains that elicit output responses (55). However, we found that the REC and HTH domain of AbiR did not bind to indole (Fig. 4c and d). Given that AbiR binds to indole with the IBR motif, we speculate that AbiR might be a dual signal transduction receptor in A. baumannii, as its REC domain may receive the stimulus of A1S_1393 to regulate target genes; this needs to be confirmed by further investigation.

In addition, BLAST searches revealed that the AbiR homologs are widely conserved in many other bacterial genera, including Acinetobacter, Escherichia, Alkanindiges, and Bradyrhizobium (Table S3). We found that the AbiR homolog from A. pittii (AbiRA.p), which has the two critical residues for indole binding, could bind strongly to indole (Fig. 6). Together, our findings not only shed new light on the regulatory mechanism of pathogenicity in A. baumannii but also provide important insights into how indole is generally sensed and responded to in new signaling pathways, which might be widely distributed in various bacterial species.


# Materials and methods


## Bacterial strains, plasmids, and growth conditions

The bacterial strains used in this study are listed in Table S5. All the bacterial strains and plasmids used in this study have been sequenced. Acinetobacter baumannii and the E. coli strains were cultured in Luria–Bertani (LB) medium (10 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, 5 g/L NaCl; pH 7.0–7.5) or LB agar (LB medium containing 15 g/L agar) at 37°C. The composition of the MP minimal medium was as follows (per liter): FeSO4 · 7H2O, 1.25 × 10−4 g; (NH4)2SO4, 0.5 g; MgSO4 · 7H2O, 0.05 g; KH2PO4, 3.4 g. The pH was adjusted to 7.0. The following antibiotics were added to the medium according to experimental needs: tetracycline (10 µg/mL), apramycin (100 µg/mL), kanamycin (50 µg/mL), and ampicillin (100 µg/mL). Indole, di-IEA, and MIA (HPLC ≥ 99%) were dissolved in DMSO to a final concentration of 100 mM as a stock solution.


## Random transposon mutagenesis

An A. baumannii mutant library was constructed by random transposon mutagenesis. Electrocompetent A. baumannii cells containing PcsuA/BABCDE-lacZ were prepared by washing three times with ice-cold sterile ultrapure water and resuspended in ice-cold sterile 10% glycerol. Electroporation of the electrocompetent cells was performed with 1 µL of the EZ-Tn5 Tnp transposon. The electroporated cells were immediately recovered after electroporation. Transformants were screened based on kanamycin resistance and β-galactosidase activity.


## Construction of in-frame deletion mutants

Gene knockout was achieved by a highly efficient CRISPR‒Cas9-based genome engineering platform in A. baumannii as previously described (56). By coupling the RecAb recombination system and the CRISPR‒Cas9 genome cleavage system, a two-plasmid genome-editing system, pCasAb/pSGAb, was used for gene deletion. The 80-bp ssDNA was used for double-strand break repair in A. baumannii ATCC 17978. The primers used for this study are listed in Table S6. Successful deletions of genes were confirmed by both PCR test and DNA sequencing. LB agar plate containing 5% sucrose was used for plasmid elimination from the mutant.


## Construction of reporter strains and measurement of β-galactosidase activity

The promoter fragments were inserted upstream of the promoterless lacZ gene in pME2-lacZ. The lacZ fusion constructs were transformed into both the wild-type and the abiS deletion mutant strains of A. baumannii ATCC17978 by electroporation to obtain the relevant reporter strains. Two hundred microliters of bacterial cultures (optical density at 600 nm [OD600] = 3.0) were collected to isolate the cells to determine the β-galactosidase activity as previously described (57). Each experiment was repeated three times in parallel.


## Protein purification and analysis

The coding regions of AbiR and other proteins were amplified with the primers listed in Table S6 and fused to the expression vector pET-28a. The fusion constructs with target genes were transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3). Cells containing the recombinant plasmids were incubated in LB medium supplemented with Kan at 37° C and allowed to grow to an OD600 of 0.6. Protein expression was induced by adding 0.5 mM isopropyl beta-D-thiogalactopyranoside, and the cells were grown overnight at 16°C. Affinity purification of 6× His fusion proteins was performed by using Ni-NTA resin (Thermal). Fusion protein cleavage with thrombin protease was conducted at 4°C overnight. The cleaved fusion proteins were eluted and analyzed by SDS‒PAGE.
