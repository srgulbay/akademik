## S9.1 Extended Molecular Synthesis (Companion to §3.3)

### S9.1.1 Detailed structural and mechanistic schema of the AbaI/AbaR axis

The founding component of the *A. baumannii* quorum-sensing (QS) network is the *abaI*/*abaR* pair identified in strain M2 by Niu and colleagues [Niu 2008]. AbaI is a LuxI-family acyl-homoserine lactone synthase predicted to be the sole AHL synthase encoded by the species; AbaR is the cognate LuxR-family transcriptional regulator [Niu 2008; Bhargava 2010; Cui 2025_2]. A *lux*-box-like sequence (CTGTAAATTCTTACAG) located ~67 bp upstream of *abaI* mediates a positive-feedback loop in which the AHL–AbaR complex activates further *abaI* transcription, producing the cell-density-dependent step characteristic of LuxI/LuxR circuits [Cui 2025_2]. A targeted *abaI*::Km mutant in strain M2 failed to produce detectable AHLs and showed approximately 40% reduction in biofilm biomass relative to wild-type. Subsequent independent reproductions confirmed reduced surface motility, attenuated virulence in *Galleria* and murine models, and increased serum susceptibility for ∆*abaI* and ∆*abaIR* derivatives [Niu 2008; Sun 2021_2; Pumirat 2024]. Phylogenetic analyses suggest that *abaI* and *abaR* were horizontally acquired, with closest similarity to homologues in *Halothiobacillus neapolitanus* rather than the related *Burkholderia* clade [Bhargava 2010].

Structural and binding studies of AbaR confirm conventional LuxR-type architecture: an N-terminal autoinducer-binding domain plus a C-terminal helix-turn-helix DNA-binding domain that engages target promoters in the AHL-bound state. Transcriptome profiling of an *abaR* knockdown identified scores of differentially expressed loci including A1S_3544, A1S_0849, A1S_3867 and A1S_3011, indicating that AbaR has a regulon well beyond *abaI* itself. The accessory regulator AbaM, encoded immediately downstream of *abaI*, acts as a brake on AHL output; loss of *abaM* increases AHL titres, surface motility and biofilm biomass, while reducing virulence — establishing it as a paradoxical attenuator that uncouples QS-driven biofilm from systemic virulence. Two further upstream modulators have been described: the LysR-type regulator ABUW_1132, which positively regulates *abaIR* transcription (its deletion causes a 16-fold drop in 3-OH-C12-HSL and switches the VIR-O virulent variant to the AV-T avirulent variant); and the CRISPR-Cas system, which can target endogenous *abaI* and thereby alters antibiotic susceptibility.

**Mechanistic schema.** AbaI condenses S-adenosyl-methionine with a 3-hydroxydodecanoyl-ACP donor to synthesise 3-OH-C12-HSL → AHL diffuses across the membrane → at threshold density it binds AbaR → AbaR-AHL homodimer recognises the *lux*-box upstream of *abaI* and additional QS-target promoters → coordinated activation of biofilm, motility, efflux and virulence regulons → AbaM and AidA provide feedback dampening, while ABUW_1132 (LysR) and (p)ppGpp/RelA provide nutritional and stress-state inputs.

### S9.1.2 AHL chemistry, stereochemistry and detection

Mass-spectrometric profiling of *A. baumannii* M2 supernatants identified N-(3-hydroxydodecanoyl)-L-homoserine lactone (3-OH-C12-HSL; also written OH-C12 or OHdDHL) as the dominant AHL, accompanied by minor amounts of unsubstituted C10-HSL and C12-HSL, 3-hydroxy-C10-HSL, unsaturated 3-oxo-C11-HSL, and C14-HSL. Subsequent surveys of clinical and environmental *Acinetobacter* isolates have shown that AHL chain length (C6–C14) and substitution patterns vary between strains and lineages but converge on medium- to long-chain 3-hydroxy species, with short-chain (C6, C8) AHLs detected at sub-nM levels in ATCC 17978 supernatants. Recent LC-MS/MS quantification in plasma from burn patients with *A. baumannii* septic shock demonstrated that 3-OH-C12-HSL is detectable in vivo, supporting clinical relevance of the signal.

Stereochemistry studies by Garner and Stacy found that, unusually for LuxR systems, AbaR responds nearly identically to (R)- and (S)-3-OH-C12-HSL (EC50 0.67 and 0.82 μM respectively) — a stereochemical insignificance that contrasts with strict R-preference in *P. aeruginosa* LasR and *P. putida* PpuR. Both groups also showed that synthetic AHL analogues bearing aromatic head-groups or triazole linkages function as AbaR antagonists in *A. baumannii* reporter strains, reducing motility and biofilm formation. These observations underpin current AbaR-targeted lead chemistry, including computational lead identification efforts and natural-product screens with diverse natural-product chemotypes (carnosol, glabridin, eugenol, peppermint and *Salvia* essential oils, rhamnolipid, fatty-acid pools, and a broader phytochemical landscape). AHL signalling is environmentally tunable: exposure to blue light reduces *abaI* transcription approximately 38-fold compared with darkness in ATCC 17978, and human serum albumin sequesters 3-OH-C12-HSL to suppress AHL output. AHL pools can also be erased enzymatically: the lactonases MomL, AaL and AidA, and the *P. aeruginosa* acylase PvdQ, all degrade 3-OH-C12-HSL and attenuate biofilm formation in *A. baumannii*.

### S9.1.3 TCS cross-talk in depth

The AbaI/AbaR axis is wired in parallel to several two-component systems whose sensors detect envelope, salt, divalent-cation and antibiotic stress.

**BfmRS.** In ATCC 19606, BfmR/BfmS expression rises in response to exogenous C6-HSL (~1.33-fold) and tracks the up-regulation of the chaperone-usher *csuA/BABCDE* operon, suggesting that QS feeds into BfmRS-mediated pilus assembly. Kim and colleagues subsequently mapped a complementary regulatory loop in which BfmRS and the AbaIR system jointly govern virulence-associated genes, with reciprocal modulation under shaking vs. static growth and AHL supplementation. Loss of the (p)ppGpp-binding RNA-polymerase cofactor DksA also de-represses *bfmR/S* and *csuC/D/E*, linking the stringent response to QS-controlled biofilm machinery. Inhibition of BfmR (RstA) by *Daniellia oliveri* phytochemicals has been proposed as a QS-targeted strategy.

**AdeRS.** This TCS controls expression of the AdeABC RND efflux pump and, in turn, both influences and is influenced by QS. AdeABC-deficient strains (∆*adeB*) show elevated AHL output, type-VI secretion and biofilm formation, particularly in bile-salt-containing media. Comparative deletion analyses of AdeABC, AdeFGH and AdeIJK in the AYE reference strain demonstrate that ∆*adeRS* shows the strongest motility phenotype (24 mm vs. 13 mm wild-type) and that AdeFGH overexpression is coupled to AHL-stimulated biofilm formation.

**A1S_2811 (CheA/Y-like hybrid).** A1S_2811 is required for AbaIR-dependent surface motility and biofilm formation in ATCC 17978, and exogenous N-(3-oxodecanoyl)-L-HSL rescues the surface-motility defect of the ∆A1S_2811 strain, providing direct genetic evidence that AHL signalling converges on chemotaxis-type signal transduction.

**PmrAB.** The PmrAB system, which governs lipid-A modifications relevant to colistin resistance, is repeatedly co-listed with *abaI*/*abaR* in transcriptomic surveys of CRAB and colistin-treated isolates, but a mechanistic link between PmrAB and the AHL circuit has not yet been established in *A. baumannii*.

### S9.1.4 Nucleotide second-messenger pools and the cAMP/c-di-GMP cascade

Three intracellular nucleotide second messengers feed into the QS network. The *A. baumannii* ATCC 17978 genome encodes 12 candidate GGDEF/EAL domain proteins that synthesise or degrade c-di-GMP; A1S_1695, A1S_2506 and A1S_3692 act as diguanylate cyclases, and A1S_1254 is a phosphodiesterase, with deletions altering biofilm, motility and virulence. The c-di-GMP effector EF-P (A1S_2419) couples the messenger to translational rescue of proline-rich proteins, providing a unique mode of QS-relevant output. (p)ppGpp synthesised by the RelA homologue ABUW_3302 (RelAAb) modulates antibiotic susceptibility, biofilm formation and virulence; importantly, RelAAb represses ABUW_1132, which itself activates *abaIR* — placing the stringent response upstream of the AHL circuit. The adenylate cyclase CavA produces 3′,5′-cAMP, which binds the Vfr-like receptor VfrAb; remarkably, in *A. baumannii* AB5075 cAMP positively (not inversely) regulates c-di-GMP and represses *abaI* transcription, placing 3′,5′-cAMP at the apex of a hierarchical cascade.

**Schema.** Environmental cue (nutrient or stress) → CavA produces 3′,5′-cAMP → VfrAb represses *abaI* while activating diguanylate cyclases → c-di-GMP rises → EF-P enhances translation of Pro-rich biofilm proteins. In parallel, nutrient starvation → RelAAb produces (p)ppGpp → represses ABUW_1132 → reduces *abaIR* transcription → biofilm/motility switch. Output: integration of cell density (AHL), intracellular energy state ((p)ppGpp), and carbon/host cue (cAMP) at a single transcriptional decision point.

### S9.1.5 Polymicrobial and inter-kingdom signalling

*A. baumannii* shares niches (lung, wound, catheter biofilms) with *P. aeruginosa*, *S. aureus* and *Candida* spp., and inter-species QS cross-talk is increasingly recognised. Bhargava and colleagues showed that the *P. aeruginosa* LasI/RhlI signals 3-oxo-C12-HSL and C4-HSL activate the *lasI* and *rhlI* promoters and modulate *A. baumannii* biofilm in mixed cultures, with reciprocal effects on AHL output. *P. aeruginosa* exerts a competitive inhibition on *A. baumannii* in co-culture; carbon/QS-based interactions and MvfR (PqsR)-dependent signalling are central, and pharmacological MvfR inhibition partially restores *A. baumannii* survival in co-culture without changing *P. aeruginosa* abundance. Conversely, indole produced by *A. baumannii* AbiS reduces competitive fitness of *P. aeruginosa* by repressing its QS systems and T3SS, defining a bidirectional signalling axis. Indole derivatives (e.g. 7-hydroxyindole) reduce *abaI*/*abaR* expression and inhibit XDR-AB biofilm, while carnosol disrupts AbiR–promoter binding with a KD of 0.3 μM and reduces biofilm, motility and cytotoxicity by 25–85% in ATCC 17978. Cross-kingdom interactions with *Candida albicans* in airway models facilitate *A. baumannii* pneumonia, plausibly via farnesol/AHL cross-talk, although the molecular interface remains uncharacterised. Finally, AHL-mediated quorum quenching by environmental and engineered lactonases reduces inter-species and intra-species signalling in mixed-species biofilms relevant to chronic-wound and ventilator-associated infections.

### S9.1.6 Detailed open questions

Several specific questions remain unresolved beyond those summarised in §3.3.7 of the main text. *First*, whether the indole QS system (AbiS synthase / AbiR receptor) operates universally across the species is unclear — most mechanistic work is in ATCC 17978, and the prevalence of functional *abiS*/*abiR* alleles in global IC1/IC2 clones has not been systematically catalogued. *Second*, no additional QS signals — equivalents of AI-2, DSF, PQS or anthranilic acid — have yet been firmly established in *A. baumannii*, and whether the species senses heterologous signals via orphan LuxR solos is unknown. *Third*, c-di-GMP effectors beyond EF-P remain unidentified; the molecular link between c-di-GMP and AbaR-target promoters has not been demonstrated structurally. *Fourth*, the PmrAB–QS interface, well described in *Salmonella* and *P. aeruginosa* for lipid-A modification and polymyxin resistance, has not been characterised in *A. baumannii*; the same applies to the relationship between QS and the σ-factor RpoN, which is known in *P. aeruginosa* to gate biofilm and motility through *fleQ* and *rhlR* but has no published *A. baumannii* equivalent in the corpus reviewed here. *Fifth*, while LasI/RhlI/PqsR–AbaR cross-talk is documented in vitro, the in vivo significance of polymicrobial AHL/AQ exchange in human infection — including the relative contributions of AHL-degrading host enzymes (paraoxonases) and exogenous QQ — remains an inferential rather than directly demonstrated component of the network. *Sixth*, although AbaR-binding consensus motifs are emerging from transcriptomic and ChIP-style work, a genome-wide AbaR binding map analogous to LasR ChIP-seq datasets in *P. aeruginosa* has not been published for *A. baumannii*. *Seventh*, the role of small regulatory RNAs and nucleoid-associated proteins (AtfA, H-NS) as bridges between QS and antibiotic-resistance regulons is suggested but mechanistically thin. Addressing these gaps — through cross-clonal mutant libraries, structural AbaR studies, in vivo signal profiling, and systems-level chemical-biology approaches — should be a priority for the next wave of QS-targeted antivirulence development against carbapenem-resistant *A. baumannii*.

## S9.2 Extended Intervention Synthesis (Companion to §3.4)

### S9.2.1 Natural products and phytochemicals — deep dive

Plant- and microbe-derived QS inhibitors dominate the literature numerically and account for 52 dedicated studies plus a further 18 that incorporate phytochemicals into multimodal platforms. Five chemotypes recur: (i) volatile terpenoids in essential oils (*Salvia abrotanoides*, *Mentha piperita*, *Ambrosia hispida*, eugenol, plus the broad survey by Wei and colleagues); (ii) phenolic diterpenes and abietanes, exemplified by carnosol; (iii) isoquinoline alkaloids, principally berberine and analogues; (iv) flavonoids/isoflavonoids such as glabridin and kaempferol; and (v) phenolic acids and small aromatics, including 3-hydroxybenzoic acid, 2,4-di-tert-butylphenol, 6-gingerol, and traditional Chinese medicine monomers.

Mechanistically, three modes recur. First, signal-mimicry and AbaR-binding antagonism: in silico docking studies repeatedly localise active phytochemicals to the autoinducer-binding pocket of AbaR or the AbaI substrate cleft. A dual-target structure-based pipeline against AbaI and AbaR prioritised six lead hits with favourable ADMET profiles. Carnosol, isolated by activity-guided HPLC fractionation, reduced AbiR binding to target promoters by approximately 10 μg/mL bioactivity and at 100 μM significantly suppressed *abaI*/*abaR*/*abiS*/*abiR* promoter activity in *A. baumannii* ATCC 17978, with rescue of *G. mellonella* survival. Second, signal degradation by polyphenol catalysis or chelation of catalytic cofactors. Third, transcriptional down-regulation of *abaI*/*abaR*: glabridin reduced *abaI* and *abaR* expression by 39.1% and 25.2% respectively (P<0.001), with 27.4% biofilm inhibition at 1/2 MIC.

Quantitative outcomes are heterogeneous but consistent in direction. *Paederia foetida* extract produced dose-dependent biofilm reduction (MIC 7.81 mg/mL; significant biomass reduction at 1/2 MIC, P<0.001) in *G. mellonella*-validated work. Peppermint extract at 2× MIC shifted 64% of clinical isolates from strong to weak biofilm phenotype (P<0.001). Agricultural-waste bioactives (furfuryl alcohol with 3-methyl-2-cyclopentenone) eradicated 52% of mature biofilm on stainless-steel surfaces at 1/2 FIC. *Glycyrrhiza glabra* extract down-regulated *abaI*, *abaR*, *bap*, and *ompA* concurrently. *Streptomyces pakalii* culture supernatants inhibited >50% of ESKAPE biofilm formation. Other reported actives include vanillin/vanillic acid, eugenol, cinnamaldehyde, carvacrol and resveratrol (MICs 16–32 µg/mL), and *Bacillus vallismortis* cell-free supernatant (83% biofilm inhibition).

Structure–activity considerations recur across reports. The diterpene scaffold of carnosol, the catechol motif of polyphenols, and the prenylated isoflavonoid core of glabridin all confer hydrogen-bond donor/acceptor patterns compatible with the AbaR ligand-binding pocket. Halogenated indole substituents (5-iodo, 5-fluoro, 6-bromo) improved MIC by approximately 8–16-fold relative to the parent indole. Eugenol retained activity across nine surveyed studies. In contrast, more polar phytochemicals such as 6-gingerol showed efflux-modulating rather than direct AbaR-binding activity. Translational hurdles include compositional variability of essential oils (chemotype seasonality, harvesting-method dependence), poor aqueous solubility, low oral bioavailability of carnosol, berberine and glabridin, absence of standardised QS-readout assays (CV026 violacein, AbaR-lacZ, *abaI* qPCR, biofilm crystal violet used inconsistently), and reliance on biofilm-staining endpoints that may not reflect in vivo persistence.

### S9.2.2 Synthetic small-molecule QSIs — deep dive

Synthetic QSIs in the corpus span four scaffolds: halogenated furanones, AHL analogues, indole derivatives, and structure-based virtual-screening hits. Stacy and colleagues established proof-of-concept that non-native N-acyl homoserine lactones can antagonise AbaR with EC50 values approaching the native (R)-3-OH-C12-HSL ligand (0.67–0.82 µM for stereoisomers) and that five screened library members reduced biofilm formation in vitro. Triazole-containing AHL mimics showed LasR antagonism with IC50 values down to 3.2 µM, providing a chemical starting point transferable to AbaR by virtue of the ~40% binding-pocket similarity. Furanone C30 showed an MIC of 64 µg/mL against the CV026 indicator strain.

The most recent translationally framed synthetic-compound work (Beasley_2025) assembled 36 compounds with ≥50% QS inhibition at ≤10 µM and prioritised levamisole, azaguanine-8, ketoprofen, indomethacin and piroxicam for antibiotic-enhancement testing. Levamisole at 1/8 MIC achieved >50% biofilm inhibition in 85% of *A. baumannii* strains and reduced *P. aeruginosa* swarming (67%), biofilm (75%) and rhamnolipid secretion (53%). Indole derivatives represent a second productive chemotype: 37 of 46 synthesised indoles displayed activity against XDR-AB (MIC 64–1024 µg/mL), with 5-iodoindole, 5-fluoroindole, 6-bromoindole and 3-methylindole the most potent (MIC 64 µg/mL) and 7-hydroxyindole retaining anti-biofilm activity at 1/64 MIC. Wheeler expanded the synthetic-QSI rationale to polymicrobial contexts by targeting the MvfR (PqsR) regulator.

Structure-based design is exemplified by BellI, whose molecular dynamics simulations identified a dual-targeting hit (hit 2) against AbaI and AbaR with favourable pharmacokinetic predictions. Aruwa applied an analogous approach to the BfmR (RstA) response regulator using *Daniellia oliveri* phytochemicals, and Alagesan targeted TetR-family regulators that converge on biofilm and antibiotic-resistance gene clusters. Phenotypic screening approaches — including Tiwari's ion-channel modulators identifying ZINC12496555 with a sub-nanomolar predicted Kd (2.2 × 10⁻⁷ M) and demonstrable styrene-sulphonate and 4-aminopyridine biofilm inhibition — complement these rational efforts but suffer from limited target deconvolution. CRISPR-Cas-mediated endogenous AbaI suppression provides genetic proof that AbaI is a tractable single-protein target. Lead-optimisation discussions remain absent for most scaffolds; only Beasley and Li articulate clear go/no-go developability metrics including solubility, cytotoxicity counterscreens (HepG2 and MCF-7), and antibiotic-enhancement triage. No synthetic AbaR antagonist has entered IND-enabling studies, primarily because of (i) cytotoxicity in eukaryotic counterscreens, (ii) limited in vivo PK data, and (iii) absence of validated *A. baumannii*-only reporter strains for SAR refinement outside a few ATCC backgrounds.

### S9.2.3 Bacteriophage therapy — deep dive

Phage therapy is the largest intervention class (n=67) and the most rapidly expanding, with 14 new isolation/characterisation reports published in 2024–2025. The 2025 isolates exemplify complementary modes. Phage vB-AbaM-fThrA is a myovirus with a 70 nm head and 90 nm contractile tail, isolated against CR-MDR *A. baumannii* and displaying a relatively broad host range with documented anti-biofilm activity. Phage vB_AbaM_AB4P2 encodes a depolymerase, produces halo-bearing plaques (4 mm clear zone, 10 mm halo) and individually reduces biofilm biomass by 52%, with mature-biofilm ablation reaching 33%. The ΦZC2/ΦZC3 pair showed plaque halos diagnostic of polysaccharide depolymerase activity, with ΦZC3 (a myovirus) reducing bacterial burden in co-cultured A549 cells by ~5 log10 over 6 h at varied MOIs without cytotoxicity. Obolenskvirus A72 (myovirus morphology; 91 nm head, 124 nm tail) showed sustained lytic activity against MDR isolates albeit a narrow host range.

Phage cocktails address host-range limitations. de Villiers de la Noue rationally combined phages 3014 and 3098 against a burn-patient isolate from Lausanne; the cocktail's calculated virulence VP=0.62 exceeded individual phage performance, and *G. mellonella* survival rose from 0% (infected control) to substantially higher values (50% and 43.75% for single phages at MOI=100; significantly higher with the cocktail, P<0.001). Wienhold reduced pulmonary *A. baumannii* burden by ~1 log10 at 24 h with single-dose phage therapy in mice. Li characterised a Saclayvirus effective in pneumonia prevention. Hydrogel-formulated phages vB_AbaM-SPA and vB_AbaM-SPB enhanced wound healing and survival in rodents. Endolysin-derived peptides PE04-1, PE04-1(NH2), and PE04-2 showed MICs of 156–313 µg/mL and >90% inhibition with colistin/citric acid, including PE04-2 at 91.9%. A phage-derived fused endolysin lysAB-vT2-fusion exhibited a five-fold improved MIC (2 mg/mL vs >10 mg/mL parent) and a FIC index of 0.25 in combination with colistin. Additional 2022–2024 phages with in vitro anti-biofilm and/or zebrafish, *G. mellonella*, or murine activity include Wintachai (Phapecoctavirus vABPW7 in A549; head 94.7 ± 8.2 nm), Mardiana (vB_AbaM_ABMM1 in zebrafish at MOI 10), Jiang (vB_AbaM-SHI; broad host range with 5–7 mm lysis zones), Peters (T4-like DLP1/DLP2 with characteristic 0.5–1 mm clear plaques), Tan (vB_AbaM_AB3P2), Erol (catheter-biofilm-active C2/K3 cocktail), Mukhopadhyay, Yadav, Wang_2022_2 (linking CRISPR-Cas to AbaI suppression), Benyamini, Cook (ddrR-mediated coregulation), and engineered/formulated systems in Yan and YuCheng (spray-dried inhalable powders for pulmonary delivery, Tg ~110 °C for trehalose-rich formulations). Styles and Sitthisak represent the closest approaches to clinical translation within the corpus. Resistance development through capsular polysaccharide diversification, gene transfer of resistance islands (Correa), and natural transformation (Godeux) all represent threats to durable phage efficacy.

### S9.2.4 Nanoparticle-formulated QSIs — deep dive

Twenty-two studies investigated nanocarriers. Staphyloxanthin-loaded niosomal nanovesicles (177.8 nm, 92.7% entrapment, 39.6% slow release) attained MIC values up to 16 µg/mL against MDR isolates, reduced biofilm by 68–88%, motility by 66.7–94.5%, siderophore production by 48.8–79.5%, and disrupted mature biofilm by 82%. Silver nanoparticles (AgNP, 10–50 nm spherical) demonstrated MICs of 4–25 µg/mL with significant anti-biofilm and anti-virulence activity. Hypericin nanoparticles co-formulated with D-tryptophan (HypNP@D-Trp) and activated by photo-sonodynamic therapy achieved synergistic FIC=0.5. Au@MSN core–shell systems with vanillic acid payloads achieved photothermal heating to 60 °C in 12 s under 808 nm irradiation. Beyond these, polymeric and lipid carriers (Siddique), chitosan or polymer-stabilised AgNP (Wintachai), silver/curcumin platforms (Pourhajibagher), and curcumin nanoplatforms (Bhowmik, Natsheh) are documented. Encapsulation rationale rests on three pillars: (i) improved penetration through biofilm extracellular polymeric substance via sub-200 nm particle size and surface charge engineering, (ii) sustained release at the infection site to maintain sub-MIC QSI exposure across multiple bacterial generations, and (iii) protection of labile cargo (essential-oil terpenoids, AHL mimics, photosensitisers) from premature enzymatic or oxidative degradation. The photosonodynamic activation paradigm in Pourhajibagher adds an external trigger, enabling spatial and temporal control of QSI release in catheter and wound contexts. Translation barriers include nanoparticle protein corona formation in physiological matrices, batch-to-batch reproducibility of size and entrapment, regulatory pathways for combination products, the lack of long-term toxicology/clearance data, and a near-universal absence of pharmacokinetic/biodistribution evidence.

### S9.2.5 Peptides and quorum-quenching enzymes — deep dive

Of 31 peptide and 30 QQ-enzyme studies, the majority remain at the in vitro stage. Endolysin-encoded peptides PE04-1, PE04-1(NH2), and PE04-2 act on the outer membrane and biofilm matrix. Octopromycin, an octopus-derived AMP, displayed MIC 50 µg/mL and MBC 200 µg/mL with collapse of biofilm architecture at sub-MIC concentrations and concomitant down-regulation of QS genes. X33 antimicrobial oligopeptide showed MIC 78.13 µg/mL and reduced biofilm formation by 23–30% at MIC fractions. The CavA-mediated cAMP–QS interface explored by Harkova highlights peptide-druggable second-messenger nodes.

QQ enzymes principally target the AHL signal. The acylase PvdQ from *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* was the first heterologous Ntn-hydrolase shown to be cross-active on the *A. baumannii* 3-OH-C12-HSL ligand. The lactonase MomL, at ≥1 µg/mL, degraded essentially all 3-OH-C12-HSL (10 µM) in vitro and increased antibiotic susceptibility of biofilms, with caveats raised about Pseudomonas/Acinetobacter assay artefacts including the ~40% baseline biofilm-formation difference between *abaI* mutant and wildtype strains. AaL is an AHL lactonase with unusually low KM (10–80 µM) relative to AiiA (~1 mM), supporting in vivo relevance at physiological signal concentrations. Aii20J QQ enzyme (20 µg/mL) was active against *A. baumannii* ATCC 17978 motility and biofilm phenotypes, with DNase co-treatment further potentiating effects. The endogenous α/β-hydrolase AidA functions as a *bona fide* QQ enzyme in clinical *A. baumannii* isolates and is itself redox-regulated, decreasing under H₂O₂ exposure with reciprocal up-regulation of *abaI*. Engineered SsoPox A variants reduced *P. aeruginosa* macrophage cytotoxicity from 80% to 20% and provided cross-species AHL hydrolysis confirming the protein-engineering tractability of the phosphotriesterase/lactonase fold. Light-modulated regulation of AbaI and host-derived signals (HSA, whole blood) reveal QQ-relevant physiological levers that may inform formulation choices for catheter-associated infections. Surface-tethered QQ activity on catheter/implant surfaces is a particularly attractive engineering route because activity can be maintained without systemic enzyme exposure, mitigating immunogenicity concerns.

### S9.2.6 Drug repurposing — deep dive

Fourteen repurposing studies target *A. baumannii* QS using approved pharmacopoeia. Nitroimidazoles (a class including metronidazole/secnidazole derivatives) were repositioned using a combined in silico, in vitro, and in vivo workflow; they suppressed biofilm and virulence in MDR *A. baumannii* with preserved pharmacokinetic advantages over de novo chemical matter. Beasley identified piperacillin combined with ketoprofen, indomethacin, and piroxicam (NSAID class) as the broadest QS-enhanced antibacterial mixture, and levamisole — an anthelmintic — as a single-agent QSI achieving >50% biofilm reduction in 85% of *A. baumannii* strains at 1/8 MIC. Seleem reported MICs of 0.5, 256, 128, 2048, and 10,000 µg/mL respectively for azithromycin, erythromycin, propranolol, levamisole and chloroquine, with sub-MIC QSI activity. Statin and NSAID repurposing — explicitly framed as low-dose QS modulation rather than antibacterial pressure — is reviewed by Zhong and Panda. The accelerated translational appeal is well documented: PK/PD parameters are largely known, safety margins are established at clinically used doses, and combination regimens with last-line antibiotics (colistin, tigecycline, sulbactam/durlobactam) offer a near-term path to clinical trial under existing regulatory pathways.

### S9.2.7 Vaccines and antibody-based approaches — deep dive

Eleven studies addressed immunological strategies. The principal targets are outer membrane vesicle (OMV) preparations, AbaI/AbaR-derived antigens, and broader virulence-factor-based subunit vaccines, several of which are themselves QS-regulated and therefore co-attenuated when QS is pharmacologically suppressed. Rajangam summarises monoclonal antibody, OMV, and capsular polysaccharide candidates. Mendes and Roy collate AbOmpA, Bap, and BfmRS as antigen sources whose expression is QS-regulated and whose surface display correlates with biofilm-mode persistence. Hetta catalogues current preclinical vaccine candidates and notes the absence of any *A. baumannii*-specific licensed immunisation product. Lazar, Panda, and Venkateswaran enumerate antibody-based blockade of QS signal reception, including conjugate constructs targeting the 3-OH-C12-HSL ligand itself. Ghavanloughajar provides relevant whole-blood-induction data informing vaccine antigen prioritisation. No randomised clinical immunisation data against *A. baumannii* QS targets appear in the included corpus.

## S9.3 Extended In Vivo and Clinical Evidence (Companion to §3.5–§3.6)

### S9.3.1 Galleria mellonella — per-study protocol detail

Peleg established *G. mellonella* as a tractable model with: killing inoculum- and temperature-dependent, with significantly greater larval mortality at 37 °C than at 30 °C (P = 0.01), and clinical *A. baumannii* strains outperforming less-pathogenic *A. baylyi* and *A. lwoffii* (P < 0.001). Critically, the authors reported that genetically reduced production of the canonical AHL signal 3-OH-C12-HSL did **not** alter virulence against *G. mellonella*, an early caution that QS-mediated phenotypes in vitro may not translate cleanly to invertebrate killing endpoints. The closely related *C. elegans* coinfection work similarly framed *A. baumannii* as a competitor in mixed-species biofilms.

Fernandez-Garcia demonstrated that an ATCC 17978 ΔabaI mutant produced 30% absolute mortality reduction at 24 h (70% versus 100% in wild type) at an inoculum of 8 × 10⁴ CFU/larva. Tang, screening 80 north-east-China clinical isolates, found that AHL-producing strains killed significantly more larvae (P < 0.01) and that *abaI*/*abaR* carriage correlated with MDR (P < 0.01). Sun reported serum sensitivity of ΔabaI and ΔabaIR (P < 0.001) and attenuated larval lethality. The phase-variable AB5075 model linked translucent and opaque colony variants to differential motility, biofilm and *Galleria* virulence (Tipton). Hypervirulent bacteraemic clinical isolates have been benchmarked, with HUMC1 reaching twice the cell density of comparators and producing the Zonula occludens toxin (Zot). The IJAA 2024 series on XDR hypervirulent community-onset clones similarly used larval and murine readouts to confirm enhanced virulence (Chen_2024). The 3014/3098 cocktail produced a striking *Galleria* readout: 86.67% larval survival at 96 h (P < 0.001 vs infection control) versus 50% and 43.75% for single phages, with phage cocktail toxicity controls confirming 80–92.5% baseline survival. Jiang's zebrafish model demonstrated that an *abaI* knock-out attenuated host cytokine induction and improved survival, providing the only vertebrate-development model in this corpus combining QS gene disruption with whole-organism readouts.

### S9.3.2 Murine models — per-study protocol detail

The Cui carnosol study: 40 BALB/c mice (6–8 weeks, randomly allocated to four groups of n = 10) received intraperitoneal *A. baumannii* ATCC 17978 (100 µL at OD₆₀₀ = 0.8) followed 4 h later by oral carnosol (100 µM) or PBS. Day-4 mortality fell from 80% (untreated) to 30% (carnosol-treated), with parallel ex vivo evidence that carnosol suppressed *A. baumannii*-induced caspase-1, ASC, NLRP3, IL-1β, IL-6 and TNF-α expression in RAW 264.7 macrophages (one-way ANOVA, P < 0.001). The authors explicitly stated adherence to ARRIVE 2.0 principles but acknowledged the sample size was **not** predetermined by statistical method.

The Wienhold pneumonia study: C57BL/6N female mice (8–10 weeks, specific-pathogen-free, randomly assigned cages) were transnasally infected with 5 × 10⁸ CFU of a clinical MDR *A. baumannii* outbreak isolate and treated 12 h post-infection with a single intratracheal aerosolisation of 5 × 10⁶ PFU/mL phage vB_AbaM_Acibel004. Phage-treated animals demonstrated approximately 1 log₁₀ CFU reduction in lung tissue at 24 h post-infection, with concordant immunohistochemical reductions in bacterial antigen, less peripheral spread on H&E sections, restoration of body temperature, and lower BAL/plasma serum albumin ratios. Histopathology was evaluated by board-certified veterinary pathologists **blinded to study group** — a rarity in this literature. Ex vivo human lung tissue confirmed no detectable CFU at 2 h post-phage treatment without significant induction of IL-1β or IL-8.

Leshkasheli demonstrated efficacy of newly isolated phages in a murine XDR *A. baumannii* bacteraemia model — survival benefit was reported but with limited disclosure of allocation concealment, blinding, or sample-size justification. The Vogel PvdQ study established proof-of-concept that an exogenous QQ enzyme could reduce biofilm formation. Elshamy evaluated hydrogel-formulated lytic phages vB_AbaM-SPA and vB_AbaM-SPB in a burn-wound model with accelerated dermal mature collagen formation, improved survival, and faster wound healing. Khafagy repurposed nitroimidazoles in a triphasic in silico/in vitro/in vivo design. Su characterised phage vB_AbaM_AB4P2 with depolymerase-mediated biofilm clearance of 52% as a single agent. The Nosair niosomal-staphyloxanthin nanovesicle platform achieved 68–88% biofilm reduction and 82% mature-biofilm disruption with associated suppression of QS gene transcripts in murine challenge. Hetta showed silver nanoparticles (MIC 4–25 µg/mL) attenuated QS-regulated phenotypes and improved survival. Wintachai isolated Phapecoctavirus vABPW7 with A549 epithelial-cell antibiofilm activity validated in larval/murine readouts.

### S9.3.3 ARRIVE 2.0 compliance — per-tool analysis

Reporting quality is uneven across the corpus. The Cui carnosol study explicitly invokes ARRIVE adherence but states that "the sample size was not predetermined using a statistical method" — a direct failure of ARRIVE essential item 2. The Wienhold pneumonia study is among the most rigorous in the corpus, with randomisation, predefined humane endpoints, blinded histopathological evaluation, and clear inclusion/exclusion criteria, but does not report formal sample-size justification. Other murine studies (Khafagy, Elshamy, Su, Nosair, Hetta, Leshkasheli) typically present survival curves and CFU data without disclosing allocation concealment, blinding of outcome assessors, sample-size calculation, or animal exclusions. Where the unit of analysis (cage versus animal) matters for cytokine or histology readouts, this is rarely specified. Effect-size estimates derived from these studies should be treated as exploratory and at substantial risk of overestimation; meta-analytic synthesis of the QSI/phage in vivo literature should incorporate domain-based risk-of-bias assessment (SYRCLE or equivalent) before any pooled effect is generated.

### S9.3.4 Carpenito 2025 — method detail

Carpenito and colleagues developed and validated an LC-MS/MS method for simultaneous determination of nine QS molecules (five N-acyl-homoserine lactones and four 2-alkyl-4(1H)-quinolones) in human plasma, with limits of quantification 0.02–0.79 ng/mL and intra-assay CV <15%. Applied to three critically ill burn patients (n = 3; Burn Unit, CTO Hospital, Italy) with hemoculture-positive septic shock sustained by multidrug-resistant *A. baumannii*, the canonical *A. baumannii* signal 3-OH-C12-HSL peaked at 1.5 ng/mL on day 1 of sampling (mean 0.8 ng/mL across three consecutive days), while C7-PQS, C9-PQS, HHQ and HQNO ranged 0.5–1.5 ng/mL with one patient peaking at 5 ng/mL on day 3. The youngest patient (85% total burn surface area) died after eight months of hospitalisation. Although the cohort is too small to support biomarker validation, the study provides the first proof-of-concept that *A. baumannii*-associated AHLs are quantifiable in human plasma during clinical sepsis and that polymicrobial AQ signatures may be detectable in the same matrix.

### S9.3.5 Genomic epidemiology — per-study granular detail

Lebreton sequenced 167 *A. baumannii* and 93 *P. aeruginosa* isolates from 223 Ukrainian patients spanning the pre- and post-2022 invasion periods, identifying highly related XDR clones (ST-2 with blaOXA-23, ST-78 with blaOXA-72 and ST-400 with blaGES-11) circulating across both periods, with hospital wound, surveillance, respiratory, burn and blood sources. The post-invasion *P. aeruginosa* ST-773 and ST-1047 carried signature LasR loss-of-function mutations — a host-adaptation signal directly relevant to QS biology — although the *A. baumannii* AbaI/AbaR locus did not show comparable accumulated loss-of-function variation. Nghiem sequenced 30 *A. baumannii* clinical isolates from two military hospitals in Hanoi, Vietnam (2022) and found ST2 (39.3%) and ST571 (21.4%) as dominant lineages. The *abaI* and *abaR* loci were present in 21 of 30 isolates and frequently co-occurred with blaOXA-23 within the same strains; physical co-localisation of blaOXA-23 and *abaR* was observed on the AC716 plasmid (n = 4), and AbaR4b resistance islands (n = 24) frequently carried both blaOXA-23 and *abaI*. Zhang studied 92 CRAB isolates from three Chinese teaching hospital ICUs (Yangzhou, Yixing, Xuyi; 2019–2022), finding near-uniform ST2/blaOXA-23 background; sputum was the source for 87 (94.6%) isolates and biofilm-formation genes (csuA-E, lpxC) were conserved. Chanchaithong extended genomic epidemiology to AbaR4-type genomic islands across *A. baumannii* clones and *A. pittii* from companion-animal infections, evidencing the One Health dimension of these resistance and virulence determinants. Meumann showed community-onset isolates were not phylogenetically distinct from nosocomial isolates. Kim_2013 mapped CRAB global clone 2 and AbaR-type resistance islands across Asia. Wiradiputra found susceptible isolates paradoxically had higher propensity for strong biofilm formation. Azimi genotyped 50 MDR clinical samples from Iranian ICUs for community-interaction genes. Sharma found colonisers had significantly stronger biofilm (P = 0.03) and desiccation tolerance (P = 0.002) than pathogenic isolates. Liu showed *abaI*- and *csuE*-positive isolates had higher MDR rates against 14 antibiotic classes (P < 0.05). Fernandez-Garcia, using 30 isolates from the GEIH-REIPI Spanish multicentre study (45 hospitals, 246 patients), demonstrated that *aidA* expression was significantly lower (P < 0.001) in bacteraemic pneumonia than non-bacteraemic pneumonia — the first direct linkage between a QS regulatory gene's expression level and a specific clinical syndrome. Ghavanloughajar showed upregulation of iron- and zinc-acquisition systems and the *csu*/QS axis under human whole blood exposure.

## S9.4 Methodological Quality Detail (Companion to §3.9)

### S9.4.1 Box 1 — Minimum reporting dataset for *A. baumannii* QS studies (full content)

> **Box 1. Minimum reporting dataset for *A. baumannii* quorum-sensing studies.**
> 1. Full strain provenance, antimicrobial susceptibility profile, capsular type and sequence type (ST).
> 2. Culture medium with cation supplementation status (Ca²⁺/Mg²⁺ explicitly stated).
> 3. Inoculum density at biofilm initiation, with growth-phase definition.
> 4. At least two orthogonal biofilm readouts (biomass + viability or biomass + architecture).
> 5. Explicit fractional MIC values for all sub-inhibitory testing, with growth-curve controls.
> 6. ARRIVE 2.0-compliant in vivo methods: randomisation, blinded outcome assessment, sample-size justification and pre-specified humane endpoints.
> 7. Deposition of raw omics data in GEO/SRA/ENA (sequencing) or PRIDE/ProteomeXchange (proteomics).
> 8. Molecular docking with decoy controls (e.g., DUD-E) and ≥200 ns molecular-dynamics simulations where structural-binding claims are central.
> 9. Deposition of negative and inactive screening data (e.g., as supplementary tables) to mitigate positive-result publication bias.

Adoption of this dataset — ideally backed by journal policy in the field's leading outlets — would impose minimal additional burden and would substantially improve the cumulative interpretability of *A. baumannii* QS research.

### S9.4.2 Per-tool framework detail

**In vitro studies (CRIS-aligned framework).** Across the 33 full-text in vitro records, recurring omissions included inoculum size at biofilm initiation; medium composition (including divalent cation concentration, which materially affects *A. baumannii* biofilm formation as shown by Chen_2019); exact wavelength and threshold used in crystal violet (CV) quantification; and rationale for selected sub-MIC concentrations of test compounds. Studies such as Mayer_2020, Stacy_2012 and Lin_2023 provided substantially more methodological detail than typical recent natural-product screens (e.g. Masoomi_2025, MajidiFard_2025, Santajit_2025), several of which reported only optical density readings without strain-specific growth curves or solvent controls.

**Animal studies (SYRCLE + ARRIVE 2.0).** Compliance was patchy. Across the *G. mellonella*, murine pneumonia, sepsis, and wound-infection studies, group sizes were almost universally reported but justification by power calculation was rare (explicit power analyses in <10% of in vivo papers). Randomisation of animals to groups was stated in roughly one-third; blinding of outcome assessment in fewer than a quarter; and attrition (deaths before endpoint, exclusions) was inconsistently disclosed. Foundational invertebrate studies (Peleg_2008, Peleg_2009) predate ARRIVE 2.0. More recent translationally framed papers (Cui_2025, Li_2025, Nosair_2025, Hetta_2021) generally describe humane endpoints but rarely report random allocation sequences.

**Computational studies (CHARMS-modified framework).** Most molecular-docking and virtual-screening papers (BellI_2025, Aruwa_2025, Koshak_2024, Masoomi_2025, Jha_2024) used a single docking engine without consensus scoring, did not include decoy controls (DUD-E, DEKOIS), and did not validate in silico hits in an orthogonal experimental cohort beyond the originating laboratory's own isolates. Cross-laboratory in silico validation was virtually absent. Where MD simulations were reported, simulation length (commonly 50–100 ns) was below the 200–500 ns increasingly considered standard for ligand-binding stability against bacterial transcription regulators.

**Omics studies (MIQE / MINSEQE).** Compliance with MIQE for qPCR confirmation experiments was variable: primer sequences and amplicon details were generally provided, but reporting of reference-gene validation, melt-curve quality, and amplification efficiency was inconsistent. For RNA-seq, dual-RNA-seq, and proteomics work (RumboFeal_2013, Liu_2014, Cui_2022, Wiradiputra_2025), MINSEQE-aligned data deposition rates were comparatively strong: roughly 70% of sequencing-based studies deposited raw reads in GEO/SRA/ENA. Proteomics data deposition (PRIDE/ProteomeXchange) was lower (~50%). Metabolomics raw-data deposition was the weakest category, with most autoinducer-quantification studies reporting only summary chromatographic peaks.

### S9.4.3 Heterogeneity sources — extended discussion

Four heterogeneity sources constrained quantitative synthesis. *Strain diversity*: ATCC 17978 dominates (n = 109 of 228 records mentioning strains, 47.8%), followed by ATCC 19606 (n = 41, 18.0%), AB5075 (n = 22, 9.6%) and AYE (n = 12, 5.3%). Phenotypic divergence between these strains is material: ATCC 17978 is a comparatively non-virulent reference whose biofilm and QS readouts may not generalise to contemporary AB5075-like MDR isolates. Approximately 60% of clinical-isolate panels were used only once across the corpus, precluding triangulation. *Biofilm assay variation*: CV staining (semi-quantitative, biomass), XTT/MTT (metabolic activity), CLSM with LIVE/DEAD (architecture and viability), and qPCR of *bfmS*/*ompA*/*csu* (transcript-level) measure different biological properties and are not interconvertible. A 50% "biofilm reduction" by CV is not equivalent to a 50% reduction by XTT. *MIC and susceptibility methodology*: CLSI versus EUCAST breakpoints, broth microdilution versus agar dilution versus E-test, and inoculum size differences yielded MIC ranges varying by up to a 2- to 4-fold dilution between laboratories for the same compound–strain pair. *Dose/concentration range*: sub-MIC QSI testing was performed at concentrations ranging from 1/2 MIC to 1/64 MIC across the corpus; reported anti-biofilm effect sizes are predictably concentration-dependent, and without standardised reporting, claims of "potent QS inhibition" at unspecified fractional MIC are not comparable.

### S9.4.4 Quality-driven down-weighting in narrative synthesis

In framing the narrative synthesis (Sections 3.3–3.6) we down-weighted four categories of report whose methodological characteristics make their contribution to effect-size claims weaker: (i) in silico-only studies without wet-lab orthogonal validation (~15 records); (ii) crystal-violet-only biofilm screens of natural extracts lacking compound characterisation, growth-curve controls or active-component identification; (iii) animal studies without group-size justification, randomisation statements or attrition reporting; and (iv) clinical-isolate studies without susceptibility profiles or sequence-type information. Down-weighting does not equate to refutation, and the qualitative direction of effects across the major thematic syntheses was robust to the inclusion or exclusion of these records.
